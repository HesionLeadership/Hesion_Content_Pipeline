# How to work with AI without becoming replaceable

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-13T11:37:00

## Summary
Seven experts share strategies for professionals to use AI tools without becoming replaceable, emphasizing that the key is using AI to augment human judgment, expertise, and decision-making rather than simply automating outputs.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The article highlights the psychological tension between skill obsolescence anxiety and professional identity preservation, suggesting that professionals who maintain metacognitive control—directing, evaluating, and contextualizing AI outputs—preserve their unique value through judgment, expertise, and accountability rather than task execution speed.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should redefine role value propositions around judgment, orchestration, and business impact rather than task execution speed. Invest in developing employees' strategic thinking and decision-making capabilities, and encourage a culture where people proactively redesign their own workflows with AI rather than waiting to be automated from above.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The rise of AI tools has created a sea of uncertainty about which skills will remain valuable and which will become obsolete. Which raises a question: How can professionals use <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence" title="AI">artificial intelligence</a> tools without becoming easily replaceable? According to experts in the field, professionals who treat AI as a replacement for thinking risk making themselves redundant, while those who use it strategically can amplify their value. What follows is seven practical approaches to working alongside AI while staying indispensable in your role.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-apply-frameworks-and-guardrails-before-analysis">Apply Frameworks And Guardrails Before Analysis</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Use AI to collate data and pull out themes, but you&#8217;re the expert. You can leverage AI to analyze large data sets quickly, but you will best be able to leverage the expertise you have gained through years of hands-on experience to identify the salient patterns that emerge, the outliers that are notable and necessary, and the places where the data doesn&#8217;t fit the aligned messaging.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Professionals who stay irreplaceable aren&#8217;t avoiding AI; they&#8217;re architecting it to amplify their judgment, not replace it. The difference is structural. Instead of asking AI what to think, you give it guardrails first: a framework, a methodology, a lens through which it must pattern-match. <br /><br />I do this by running assessment data and client interview transcripts through a structured prompt that applies my EVOLVE framework definitions upfront. [The framework is <strong>E</strong>xecution and delivery; <strong>V</strong>ision and strategy; <strong>O</strong>rganizational intelligence; <strong>L</strong>eadership and influence; <strong>V</strong>alue translation; and <strong>E</strong>xpertise and Methodology.] Do people apply proven frameworks and recognize patterns across situations?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This means the AI is pattern-matching against my actual framework, not generic business categories. That early-stage pattern spotting serves a strategic purpose: It lets me stress-test my initial hypotheses before I talk to the client. I can ask the AI, &#8220;Does the data support my read on their leadership bottleneck, or am I missing something?&#8221; This is where expertise guards against confirmation bias. I&#8217;m not accepting the AI&#8217;s patterns as truth; I&#8217;m interrogating them.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The real differentiator is outlier detection. AI spots the outlier; expertise knows why it matters and what to do about it. When assessment responses point to one pattern, but the client&#8217;s stated priorities point elsewhere, that gap is a conversation starter, not a data error. Creating communication points backed by data is real value.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">AI-assisted pattern work becomes an input to my diagnostic report, but I make the final decision on what fits, what needs scrutiny, and how it surfaces to the client. The report reflects expertise, not the tool&#8217;s output. That&#8217;s the irreplaceability.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/sarahleasmith">Sarah Smith</a>, Chief Innovation Officer, <a href="https://iconoclastltd.com">Iconoclast Innovations, LLC</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-leverage-insight-then-make-the-call">Leverage Insight, Then Make The Call</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The professionals who get replaced are the ones who are using AI to produce a specific deliverable. The ones who won&#8217;t are using it to enhance their judgment or to provide insight into a decision that they, not the AI, will ultimately make. I use AI like a super analyst, and then layer on context that others don&#8217;t have, like taste, data, trend analysis, and accountability for the outcome.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A specific example: as a go-to-market adviser, I&#8217;ll use Claude to build a 100-company target list and draft five positioning angles in an afternoon. This work used to take over a week. But that&#8217;s not what I&#8217;m paid for. The value is knowing which 10 of those accounts are actually worth a call, why a buyer will or won&#8217;t trust one of those five messages, and owning the result when it ships.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/dougmesser">Doug Messer</a>, CMO/cofounder, <a href="https://www.faradex.ai">Faradex</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-orchestrate-work-let-models-handle-execution">Orchestrate Work. Let Models Handle Execution</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Be the orchestrator, not the operator. The work that gets displaced is the commoditized, transactional stuff that was never really yours, and the work moves up to a higher level. So the move is to hand AI that lower layer and make yourself the person directing it.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Here&#8217;s my own example. I use [Claude] Cowork every 10 minutes, and I&#8217;ll be honest, at least 25% of what I do as a CEO sucks. The decks, the Excels, the proposals. AI does that faster than me. But it can&#8217;t give the clear instructions, remove the roadblocks or make the judgment call, right? That&#8217;s the same muscle as managing a great team. The person who learns the latest models and builds them into how they work gets a leg up. The one with their head in the sand gets replaced.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/ozrashid">Oz Rashid</a>, founder and CEO, <a href="https://www.talentmsh.com">MSH</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-pressure-test-positions-to-sharpen-judgment">Pressure Test Positions To Sharpen Judgment</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">AI is not about going faster. It is about thinking more deeply.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The professionals most at risk from AI are not the ones who ignore it; that was a 2025 problem. They are the ones who use it to enhance their expertise and experience.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Speed is not protection from being downsized. A faster version of a replaceable output is still a replaceable output.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The one strategy I keep returning to is to start using AI to pressure-test your thinking, not to generate it. To stretch and grow. To create. And allow your &#8220;AI intern&#8221; to be a second set of eyes and ask it to challenge your thinking and positioning.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">How? Before drafting a recommendation or a point of view, develop your own position first. Write it out. Commit to it. Then bring your agent (AI) to the party. Not to write it for you, but to interrogate it. Ask the model to argue the opposite. Ask it to find the weakest assumption in your draft. Then decide whether you still hold your position, and why.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">What you are left with is not an AI output. It is a human judgment that has been pressure-tested, fact-checked and challenged. This is a human decision that carries the specific nuance of your experience, your professional context, and your willingness to defend a conclusion the model would have likely watered down.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">An executive HR leader I worked with was preparing a board recommendation on workforce redesign. Her instinct was to present three balanced options and let the board decide. Instead, we drafted her actual recommendation first. This recommendation was built by her experience, including success and the lessons learned from failures. We then used multiple AI agents to build the strongest possible cases against it. The models found two vulnerabilities she had not addressed. (This is to be expected in a world moving faster by the moment.) She worked through both and arrived at the board meeting with a single position rather than three options and uncertainty.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The board approved it.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">She created the input. AI made the recommendation better. Her judgment made it hers.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The professionals who become irreplaceable in an AI-augmented environment will not be the ones who learned to prompt well. The leaders who stand out will be the ones who learned to think more precisely because tech is showing us where the holes and flaws might be.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/staciebaird">Stacie Baird</a>, Chief Human Experience Officer, <a href="https://www.hxcoach.com">The Hx Coach</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-automate-yourself-and-control-the-system">Automate Yourself And Control The System</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I&#8217;d say replace your own job before someone else does it.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It might sound a little backwards at first. But most people are using AI defensively, under pressure, automating a few tasks and hoping nobody looks under the hood. The truth is that someone will eventually look, and chances are you&#8217;ll be told, not asked, about what comes next.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Let&#8217;s say you&#8217;re a project manager. Start with mapping out your job the way an AI consultant would if they were hired to automate it, then build the actual workflow. Like build an intake agent connected to your project tracker and team chat that monitors ticket movement and thread sentiment all week. Let that agent feed off to a risk-scoring agent that compares sprint velocity against historical baselines and flags anything that&#8217;s a risk. Then let a third agent take those flags, pull the context, and draft your Monday stakeholder update in your voice, routed to you for review before anything goes out.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">You can wire something like this together with a no-code agent orchestration platform over a couple of weekends with zero engineering support. Then demo it to your boss yourself. And voila! You went from the person writing status reports to the person who owns the orchestration layer deciding what the agents handle and where the human-in-the-loop checkpoints sit.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Nobody automates away the person who built the automation, because you&#8217;re the one who knows where it breaks and what still needs a human.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/bytanutiwari">Tanu Tiwari</a>, founder &amp; coach, <a href="https://theconsciousleaderco.com">The Conscious Leader Co.</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-prioritize-business-impact-over-delivery-speed">Prioritize Business Impact Over Delivery Speed</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I think all of our jobs are evolving. As a data science leader, my whole career has been about building data and machine learning models. The time for development has shrunk a lot—I used to pride myself on being able to create solid yearly roadmaps for my teams because I knew what the business wanted, how complex the solutions were and how long it took to build those models.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But not anymore! I don&#8217;t know what my team is going to be working on three months from now, because anything we think of can be built in a tenth of the time it took before.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A good way managers and leaders in data roles could use AI for coding, modeling and project managing without becoming replaceable is to focus on business impact. Help your sales team get more sales, product team improve your product, and <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> team identify your ideal customer groups.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Aligning yourself with your business teams&#8217; goals and taking accountability for the results is a good way for data leaders to grow their presence in the company. And it&#8217;s never been easier to do so.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/vinayakmitty">Vin Mitty, PhD</a>, Senior Director of Data Science &amp; AI, <a href="https://www.legalshield.com">LegalShield</a></p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-use-a-thought-partner-for-decisions">Use A Thought Partner For Decisions</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The people who get replaced use AI to do their old job faster. The people who don&#8217;t, use it to do a job they couldn&#8217;t do before.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Here&#8217;s a specific example. I teach teams to stop using AI like a search engine and start using it as a thought partner. One habit: Every morning, spend 10 minutes briefing your AI on the day&#8217;s decisions, not just your tasks. Tasks are easy to automate. Judgment is not.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I do this myself. I build with Claude Code every day. It writes the code. I decide what&#8217;s worth building and why. That second part is the part no model can take from you.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">So don&#8217;t just learn the tools. Use them to climb up a level. Let AI handle the execution. You own the taste, the context, and the call. That&#8217;s what keeps you in the room.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/timcakir">Tim Cakir</a>, Chief AI Officer &amp; founder, <a href="https://aioperator.com">AI Operator</a></p>
