# Morning Briefing — July 13, 2026

## Top Story
**Every Organization Has Two Realities** (HBR) — This is the story that should stop every CHRO and COO mid-coffee. The gap between the organization leaders think they're running and what employees actually experience is the root cause of failed transformations, disengagement, and culture erosion. For Hesion's audience, this is the foundational problem: if you can't close the perception gap, nothing else you invest in — AI adoption, performance management redesign, innovation culture — will land the way you intend. This is Pete's wheelhouse and a perfect anchor for any conversation with a senior leader who thinks things are going well because the dashboard says so.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**The 'Job-Hugging' Trend Is Cracking: Why Half of Employees Are Now Looking to Leave** (Inc) — This has the surprise factor and the contrarian insight that performs well on LinkedIn. Most leaders spent the last 18 months looking at low attrition numbers and exhaling. This story reframes that as a dangerous misread. **Suggested hook:** "Your retention numbers looked great last year. That wasn't loyalty — it was fear. And the dam is about to break."

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**Performance Management Needs New Metrics in the AI Era** (HBR) — This deserves the deep treatment. There's a rich org psych vein here: attribution theory (who gets credit when AI co-produces the output?), procedural justice (do employees perceive the new metrics as fair?), and motivation crowding (does measuring AI-augmented output change how people approach their work?). Pair this with the DBS Bank story on embedding innovation as a KPI and you have a newsletter theme around "the performance management system as your most powerful culture lever — and why most companies are getting it wrong right now." Pete can bring original perspective on what fair evaluation looks like when the human contribution is harder to isolate.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Leaders at All Levels: How DBS Bank Makes Everyone an Innovator** (MIT Sloan Review) — This is a clean, specific, citable case study. DBS made innovation 20% of every team's performance review. It illustrates the principle that **what gets measured gets managed — but also that structural incentives shape cultural identity**. In a workshop, Pete can use this to spark discussion: "If you made innovation a formal KPI tomorrow, would it unleash creativity or just generate performative compliance?" It's a perfect launching point for exploring the tension between extrinsic incentives and intrinsic motivation, and it gives participants something concrete to react to rather than abstract theory.

## Skip These
- **The Office Amenity That Actually Improves Teamwork** (HBR) — Despite the high score, this is a workplace design story likely centering on a single environmental variable (green space, natural light, etc.). It's interesting but too narrow and tactical for Pete's CHRO/COO audience. Save it for a social media share if the specific finding is surprising, but it won't drive a meaningful leadership conversation.
