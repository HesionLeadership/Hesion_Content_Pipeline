# LLMs like ChatGPT often prioritize Western moral values, research shows

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-16T08:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91573572/llms-chatgpt-bias-western-moral-values-research](https://www.fastcompany.com/91573572/llms-chatgpt-bias-western-moral-values-research)

## Summary
Research published in PNAS shows that large language models like ChatGPT systematically prioritize Western moral values (e.g., care, individual rights) over non-Western values (e.g., purity, authority), even when prompted to respond as citizens of non-Western nations, raising concerns about cultural bias in AI-assisted decision-making.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
Organizations deploying AI tools for cross-cultural work—conflict resolution, international team collaboration, content moderation, or HR policy—risk perpetuating Western-centric moral frameworks that misrepresent employees' and stakeholders' actual values, undermining inclusion and trust in multicultural workplaces.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs integrating AI into global operations should audit AI-generated recommendations for cultural moral bias, especially in areas like employee relations, cross-cultural training, and international communications. Relying on AI without cultural calibration can alienate non-Western employees and partners, eroding psychological safety and perceived fairness.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Large language <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> models, such as <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/chatgpt">ChatGPT</a>, often misjudge what people outside the West might value as a moral priority, according to our new research <a href="https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2519941123">published in the <em>Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</em></a>.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In 2024, we asked OpenAI’s GPT-3.5, GPT-4 and GPT-4o models to estimate the moral norms—shared ideas about right and wrong—of 48 nations and then compared them with a global sample of over 90,000 human participants. Both humans and <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence" title="AI">AI</a> models were asked to complete a <a href="https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2023-99083-001">moral foundations questionnaire</a>, in which we measured the extent to which they endorsed six moral values. These foundations were care, equality, proportionality (rewarding individuals relative to their contribution), loyalty, authority, or respect for legitimate authorities, and purity (concern with preserving what is seen as natural or sacred).</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Participants were asked to rate how much they agreed with some moral statements. For example, to assess how much someone is concerned about purity, they evaluated statements such as “I think the human body should be treated like a temple, housing something sacred within” and “It upsets me when people use foul language like it is nothing.” AI models were then prompted to respond to the same statements as an “average citizen” from each of the 48 nations represented in the sample.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Previous research by psychologist <a href="https://www.umass.edu/psychological-brain-sciences/about/directory/mohammad-atari">Mohammad Atari</a> demonstrates that <a href="https://doi.org/10.1037/pspp0000470">moral priorities across the world vary</a>: Western societies tend to place greater emphasis on concerns such as individual rights and care, whereas several non-Western societies assign relatively greater importance to values such as purity. Notably, we found a similar calibration in AI models, with them systematically emphasizing values such as care, while placing less emphasis on values such as purity.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Additionally, these models overestimated the broad moral concerns of Western nations, such as the U.S. and Australia, while underestimating those of several non-Western nations, such as Morocco and Nigeria. In other words, even when prompted to respond as an average citizen of a particular country, the models systematically aligned more with Western patterns of moral values. This finding is consistent with earlier research showing GPT’s “psychology” <a href="https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/5b26t_v1">as more aligned with Western individuals</a>.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-does-it-matter">Why does it matter?</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Generative AI is increasingly used for a wide range of tasks across cultures, including education, therapy, communication, and even policy decisions.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There is a real risk of cultural bias if AI assumes the whole world, ranging from Argentina and Egypt to Japan and Zimbabwe, ought to pursue the same values as the Western world.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Imagine that an AI model helps draft public health messaging during a pandemic, moderates online content, translates a poem, or advises a company working across cultures. In each case, the system needs some model of what people care about: what is considered harmful, fair, disrespectful, or sacred.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Our findings suggest that generative AI centers moral values in ways that are not consistent with those outside the Western world. This systematic inaccuracy, which scholar <a href="https://eccles.utah.edu/team/jesse-graham/">Jesse Graham</a> and his team refer to as “<a href="https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0050092">moral stereotyping</a>,” could lead to critical cultural missteps with real-world consequences.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For instance, imagine users asking for advice on interpersonal conflicts or looking for feedback on work collaboration with international partners. In such situations, AI models may give advice or offer language that reflects mainly Western values while overlooking those that are most important in other cultures. This could perpetuate cultural biases or lead to conclusions that are not aligned with the perspectives of those from non-Western backgrounds.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In short, if AI models misrepresent “human” values, they can amplify existing cultural blind spots and even create new disparities.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-we-don-t-know">What we don’t know</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">While our research shows that GPT models inaccurately retrieve the moral profiles of non-Western nations, important questions remain.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">First, it remains unclear whether these patterns appear in newer models or models training in languages other than English.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Second, the reasons for these moral distortions are not well understood. Models learn about the world through language, with much of their training data sourced from the internet, which is more accessible in the Western, English-dominant world. This is a plausible explanation for our results, but it needs to be tested directly.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Third, it is not yet known whether these moral biases appear outside survey settings. Moral values shape decisions in fields where AI is increasingly being used, including education, health, communication, and workplace settings.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Future studies may also need to test whether AI systems make similar errors in practice.</p>
