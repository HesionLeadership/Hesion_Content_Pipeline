# Why have people historically quit their jobs? The real reason comes down to 4 words

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-15T09:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91572140/why-people-quit-their-jobs-4-words](https://www.fastcompany.com/91572140/why-people-quit-their-jobs-4-words)

## Summary
The article argues that the primary reason employees quit their jobs boils down to four words: 'People are not valued.' It emphasizes that managers have direct influence over the key drivers of turnover, including lack of respect, limited growth opportunities, and failure to develop employees.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The article reinforces decades of research on perceived organizational support (POS) and leader-member exchange (LMX) theory—when employees feel undervalued by their direct managers, disengagement and turnover intention rise sharply. The emphasis on the manager as the linchpin of retention aligns with Gallup's well-documented finding that people leave managers, not companies.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should treat manager selection and development as their highest-leverage retention strategy. Invest in training managers to demonstrate genuine valuing behaviors—active listening, career development support, obstacle removal, and recognizing employees as whole people—rather than relying on compensation or benefits alone to retain talent.

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It’s a familiar rhythm. Just like many employees around the world, they go through a detailed interview process, taking weeks to find the perfect match through vetting and being vetted. Once they accept the offer, there’s a sense of hope and anticipation as they meet their <a href="https://www.inc.com/marcel-schwantes/middle-managers-burned-out-burnout-leadership/91332201" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">new boss</a> and colleagues.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Then, the honeymoon phase wears off, and something almost expectantly happens at every job: The manager does something (or <em>doesn’t do</em> something) that makes the new employee <a href="https://www.inc.com/marcel-schwantes/warren-buffett-hiring-top-talent-characteristics.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">question their integrity</a> and whether they really <em>give a damn</em> about people.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If you’re that new employee with hopes for a bright future, you start to doubt and wonder if this is really the same company or&nbsp;manager you initially signed on with. From there, things spiral downward as you finally realize you’re working for a person (or a group of people) who has&nbsp;no idea how to lead you&nbsp;or others.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I’ll leave the rest of that story for you to finish, since it’s very personal for many. The outcome could go in several different directions. One is to simply quit.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-most-research-on-employee-turnover-says-the-same-thing">Most research on employee turnover says the same thing</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">To this day, nothing has changed since former Gallup CEO Jim Clifton (now chairman) identified the true reason why people quit.&nbsp;</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The single biggest decision you make in your job—bigger than all the rest—is whom you name manager. When you name the wrong person manager, nothing fixes that bad decision. Not compensation, not benefits—nothing.</p>
</blockquote>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Employees across all job types, levels, and industries are ready to leave if their <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/bosses" id="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/bosses" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank" type="link">boss</a> fails to fulfill their responsibilities. Honestly, this is a commendable choice to prevent the health hazards associated with working under a toxic manager.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most of the main reasons people leave boil down to issues like:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Employees feel their companies prioritize profits or revenue over how people are treated.</li>



<li>They leave, or consider leaving, their <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/jobs" id="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/jobs" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank" type="link">jobs</a> when they dislike their direct supervisors.</li>



<li>They leave, or consider leaving, because they believe their employers don’t recruit or keep high-performing workers.</li>



<li>Their companies currently lack sufficient growth opportunities to sustain them in the long term.</li>



<li>They would be more satisfied if employers better utilized their skills and abilities.</li>



<li>They needed to leave their current companies to advance their careers.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And, as you can see, managers have direct influence over every one of these causes for quitting. So, what gives?</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-people-quit-really-boils-down-to-four-words">Why people quit really boils down to four words</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><em>People are not valued.</em></p>
