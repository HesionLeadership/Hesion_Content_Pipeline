# Stripe is the most underrated—and influential—$159 billion company in the world

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-16T09:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91538341/stripe-most-underrated-influential-159-billion-company-power-map](https://www.fastcompany.com/91538341/stripe-most-underrated-influential-159-billion-company-power-map)

## Summary
Stripe's $159 billion success is attributed to its distinctive organizational culture built on urgency without aggression, rigorous writing practices, customer obsession, and a bottoms-up innovation model that has produced 268 alumni founders and influenced AI, fintech, and climate tech.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
Stripe exemplifies how psychological safety combined with high performance standards creates an 'idea factory'—where cooperative rigor (not antagonistic evaluation) enables candid feedback, bottoms-up innovation, and entrepreneurial identity formation among employees. The Collisons' leadership style of modeling urgency through participation rather than directive pressure illustrates how leader behavioral modeling shapes organizational norms and intrinsic motivation.

## Leadership Lesson
Build a high-performance culture by pairing demanding outcome expectations with psychological safety and cooperative feedback norms: hire for 'altitude shifting' (strategic vision plus granular execution), institutionalize rigorous writing to force clarity of thought and reduce politics in decision-making, and let leaders feel operational pain firsthand rather than relying on filtered reports. CHROs should note that mission framing ('increase the GDP of the internet' vs. 'payments API') dramatically expands the talent pool you can attract.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When <a href="https://stripe.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Stripe</a>&nbsp;moved into its first office in Palo Alto about 15 years ago, its&nbsp;founders (brothers Patrick and John Collison) did what every tiny startup does: They drove to Ikea to buy cheap desks and chairs. As they started to unload their haul in their new space, though, they unwittingly revealed one of the traits that would lead Stripe to revolutionize internet payments, company formation, and more.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">After they put down some of the boxes, they started sprinting back to the car. Darragh Buckley, who was Stripe’s first employee and experienced this moment firsthand, recalls thinking,&nbsp;<em>Did&nbsp;we park illegally?</em>&nbsp;</p>

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">As Buckley, who worked at Stripe until 2016 and is now the founder and CEO of <a href="https://increase.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Increase</a>—which offers ambitious technology companies such as Stripe, Gusto, and Ramp API-first banking, the backbone that enables them to process billons (or more) annually—explains: “It’s all a pleasantly shown sense of urgency. It&#8217;s not, ‘Why haven&#8217;t you done this yet?’ It’s like, ‘Oh, come on, let&#8217;s go do this together.’ . . . <h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-stripe-s-mission-is-a-talent-magnet-and-what-stripe-wants-in-a-great-hire">Why Stripe’s mission is a talent magnet—and what Stripe wants in a great hire</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Ben Guo&nbsp;wasn’t&nbsp;sure Stripe would be a fun place to work when he joined in 2015. “Payments&nbsp;seems&nbsp;boring from the outside,” he tells me. But the reason Guo stayed for eight and a half years, leaving in 2023, is that he quickly got obsessed with the big idea that drives Stripe: “It is a decades-long vision to build infrastructure for the internet economy,” explains Guo, who’s now cofounder and president of&nbsp;<a href="https://www.zo.computer/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Zo Computer</a>, which gives users an AI computer in the cloud in pursuit of&nbsp;his equally big goal to make computing fun again and usurp control from the techno-feudalist kings. “It just felt like this whole,&nbsp;very large&nbsp;civilizational project, which was cool.”&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Guo is not alone in this belief. “Stripe could have been an &#8216;API for payments&#8217; company,” says Taylor Francis, CEO of&nbsp;<a href="https://watershed.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Watershed</a>, a platform for enterprises to manage their carbon footprints, and a Stripe alum who worked there between 2014 and 2019 and was part of the team that built Atlas. But, as he notes, “it&#8217;s easier to start a more ambitious company than a less ambitious company.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">By framing Stripe&#8217;s mission in what has now been refined as&nbsp;<em>increasing the GDP of the internet</em>, it “attracted people who may not have wanted to work on a payments API,” Francis continues, “but did want to dedicate their time, sweat, and ingenuity to economic infrastructure for the internet.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Specifically,&nbsp;here’s&nbsp;what&nbsp;can get you&nbsp;hired at Stripe.</strong>&nbsp;“We’d be looking for someone that had a big vision, but who was also a doer,” says Anthony Kline, who started and led leadership recruiting at Stripe as well as working on M&amp;A. He&#8217;s now a partner at&nbsp;<a href="https://www.thegp.com/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">TheGP</a>&nbsp;and founder and co-lead of&nbsp;<a href="https://www.xtripe.vc/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Xtripe Syndicate</a>, a group of about 750 former Stripes, as employees are known, who back startups from fellow alums, though not exclusively. (The syndicate is a subset of a larger Stripe alumni Slack community of about 2,300 people.)</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“[We wanted] folks that had driven critical-path, high-impact projects,” Kline continues, “and could go into absolute detail about what they did, how they did it, why they did it, who was involved, which other teams interacted, who the stakeholder was, what their motivation was. It did not allow for ambiguity or any lackadaisicalness when it came to why you were building something.”&nbsp;&nbsp;<br />&nbsp;<br />That kind of talent, people who could “altitude shift” (Kline’s phrase) from strategic priorities to weedy details, combined with the company’s big vision, would unlock new products, revenue, and influence.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-winning-ethos-we-haven-t-won-yet">A winning ethos: ‘We haven’t won yet’</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The other critical—and distinct—elements of Stripe’s culture are kindness and humility.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The idea of the benevolent billionaire may&nbsp;run counter to current public sentiment about the world’s richest business leaders, but&nbsp;numerous&nbsp;former employees referred to both the Collisons and the company culture in this way.&nbsp;“John and Patrick were not just brilliant, but&nbsp;really high&nbsp;EQ and thoughtful. They treated people well,” says Adam Stevenson, who worked at Stripe from 2015 to 2021 and is now president of Thatch, a personalized corporate health benefits platform. “The best people are drawn to a place where they can do their best work and have a high degree of psychological safety. Who&nbsp;doesn&#8217;t&nbsp;want to work in an environment like that?”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">One key aspect of the Stripe culture, says Meghan Scanlon, who joined in 2020 as an early enterprise sales hire, is &#8220;&#8216;we haven’t won yet.&#8217; . . . There&#8217;s a constant state of improvement, iteration, and always learning.” Scanlon, who’s now CEO of <a href="https://www.aleoop.io/">Aleoop</a>, an AI-native platform to connect frontline conversations to the teams prioritizing what to build next and why, adds that this mindset started “at the tippy top” and “permeated down in the best way possible to make sure that everyone stayed hungry and humble.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">However, one should not confuse kindness with softness. As you’ll see, Stripe’s workplace can sound intense at times. But just like the story of the Collisons running to their car to get the next set of furniture boxes, Stripe’s hunger for success is more cheerful than cutthroat.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-putting-rigor-into-writing-nbsp">Putting rigor into writing &nbsp;</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Several&nbsp;Xtripes&nbsp;describe the company to me as being an “academic place” or “university-like.” When I reference the Collisons’ propensity to share books they’re reading on X or on podcasts, multiple people tell me that Patrick and John&nbsp;really do have&nbsp;stacks of books—and&nbsp;they’ve&nbsp;read them all, too.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This academic-like rigor manifests most clearly in Stripe’s writing culture, something the Collisons adapted from Amazon. The company’s prioritization of long-form writing also ties directly into its long-term vision. &nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“‘We want to build a 50-year company, and we need to document everything’” is what Patrick told Dave Nunez during his interview process in 2017. Nunez, who is a technical writer (and co-author of <em><a href="https://www.amazon.com/Docs-Developers-Engineers-Technical-Writing/dp/B0GH5XQF99/ref=sr_1_1?crid=26F7Q975SURN4&amp;dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.dPlOj5sjvxZMc6BImDYihg_4Fnx21JMNpnwbsSjBDmjQR0oSFP0i5Rh58uyB8SoAqZ_7-CuylLhP-N9qpUIgpXYQd3xz5-GHhqjl2Z1YgqnwJvEg0A20ZvYd5_9yyzGrjoTv2owYpG_NRVXsW4GMT-3YtnnCfrIIVTjmMwTkAFPccyfwumhplCJB3RYZuFJh-b9yekbJyh8n0iIYHrn70p5VvrW1Bok8-Dt_r_8i7wo.svuTyMTCQxjfTi8TKWwEvW-xiAPZirWeGFSom619gQ8&amp;dib_tag=se&amp;keywords=docs+for+developers&amp;qid=1784121940&amp;sprefix=docs+for+d%2Caps%2C119&amp;sr=8-1">Docs for Developers</a></em>), joined Stripe to build its internal and external documentation systems and now, as&nbsp;CEO of&nbsp;<a href="https://falconer.ai/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Falconer</a>, has created a platform for companies to get the most out of their knowledge and documentation, including an AI-powered&nbsp;knowledge&nbsp;base. Stripe, which also values transparency, wants everything not only written down but accessible within the company.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When I ask Buckley, employee No. 1, if the goal was to have the equivalent of a dissertation that you’d ultimately defend, he tells me no. “It’s us against the problem,” he says. “It’s cooperative as opposed to antagonistic. There are prerequisites for that. You need to be in a highly trusted environment.” He likens it to Pixar’s brain trust, “experienced practitioners giving frank feedback on the progress,” Buckley adds. “You can do a lot more with early ideas in that environment than you can do with an antagonistic environment.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Trusted and cooperative? Sure. Also: Brace yourself.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“Were you sweating and wringing your hands when you publish something?&nbsp;Absolutely,” recalls Kline, the investor.&nbsp;“It’s&nbsp;1:00 AM or whatever, and you start seeing Patrick or John scrolling through your particular doc. It felt good that they were looking at it, but it was also nerve-wracking.” Nunez likens sharing your writing at Stripe to being “a puppy and&nbsp;you’re&nbsp;just getting smothered to death. People&nbsp;won’t&nbsp;leave you alone.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But for Stripe, this process was crucial. “Preparing for quarterly business reviews or product things, you knew that what you put forward was going to be thought through very rigorously,” says Erika Reinhardt, an engineering manager from 2017 to 2019 and cofounder and executive director of <a href="https://www.sparkclimate.org/">Spark Climate Solutions</a>, a nonprofit focused on shortening the timeline for managing major climate risks that currently lack comprehensive solutions such as methane emissions from livestock enteric methane and warming-induced emissions. “That then raises the bar across the organization of how much thought and effort people are putting into things, recognizing that it&#8217;s not just going to get the quick, easy stamp.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Or, as Stripe Press’ commissioning editor Tamara Winter puts it: “Writing is just the most useful communication technology that has ever been created. It&#8217;s easy to bullshit your way through things if you never actually have to test the idea.”</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-customer-obsession-feeds-the-idea-machine">Customer obsession feeds the idea machine</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stripe’s commitment to listening to customers informs employees’ thinking, writing, and ultimately, the ideas they pursue. “It’s crazy how rare that still is in tech,” says Falconer’s Nunez, who before starting his company “went to about 25 to 50 of my most curmudgeonly engineering friends” to test the idea. “There’s this Steve Jobs ethos where you’re better than the user.”&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">That&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;how things work at Stripe. “Your users come first, Stripe comes second, and then your team, and then it’s, like, you and then it’s your manager,” says Zo’s Guo. He adds that this is also a factor in Stripe’s culture. “By talking to users and attracting people who are into talking to users, you attract people who are generally kind and nice.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Anurag Goel, who was hired as employee No. 8 after being an early beta user of Stripe, sharing feedback, and even dropping the product to go with a rival payments processor before he ultimately joined the team, says <strong>the key wasn’t just talking to customers but listening to the&nbsp;<em>right</em>&nbsp;customers.</strong> “We developed a culture of recognizing the most valuable customers, even if they were not paying us a lot,” says Goel, who’s now CEO of <a href="https://render.com/">Render</a>,&nbsp;which operates a cloud platform for developers to host and scale applications and agents. “The value that they provided was sometimes that they were an influencer in their community, or they just gave us really useful feedback because they were using Stripe for a use case that was still nascent but that we knew was going to become big.”&nbsp;Render brings this idea forward by providing personalized support even to the free users of its cloud platform.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This obsession, though, requires constant vigilance. “A lot of us would get messages in the middle of the night,” Nunez recalls, “some Twitter&nbsp;egg&nbsp;with no followers is complaining about Stripe. We would be expected not only to address the issue if it&nbsp;was&nbsp;legit but&nbsp;respond&nbsp;to this person.” The goal was to find and deal with these complaints <em>before</em> Patrick dropped the link in Slack asking you to look into it.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Sometimes, the job at Stripe is to take the role of the user yourself, writing what are known as <em>friction logs</em>. “You go through some workflow like a customer would,” Nunez explains, sharing that an example would be something like sending an invoice from Stripe. &#8220;You&#8217;d start from signing up for Stripe,&#8221; he says. &#8220;There are dozens of steps between point A and point B. You would write a memo of every single piece of friction that you experienced along the way.&#8221;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Every identified pain point would not only be addressed but they could also create insights into what to build next. Aleoop’s Scanlon<strong>&nbsp;</strong>describes her sales role at Stripe as being “a good detective,” piecing together sometimes disparate clues to figure out how the company could solve a customer&#8217;s payment challenges. As she won deals, “because we were at the core of seeing payments in and out,” she says, “we could move up the stack, which is,&nbsp;<em>Hey, what does it look like before that customer&#8217;s customer decides to pay?</em>” That allowed Stripe to&nbsp;innovate&nbsp;the shopping cart experience, the conversion funnel, the checkout flow, and payment options.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stripe, Scanlon adds, could also go “all the way down the stack to the back office.” Meaning, it could automate such things as revenue reconciliation and subscription logic for customers. “There was a very natural progression of expanding the platform with payments still at the core.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The obsession with listening to customers combined with the writing-first culture where&nbsp;all that customer feedback got turned into written reports shared with the whole company “created this awesome dynamic,” says Nunez. “Every other company I worked for,&nbsp;it’s&nbsp;my opinion versus your opinion, and the person with the best persuasive argument or&nbsp;who’s&nbsp;the most senior gets the decision made. At Stripe,&nbsp;you’re&nbsp;only arguing over your interpretation of what the user says. You have these reports, you have the data, and if that&nbsp;was&nbsp;unclear, you would have to&nbsp;go talk&nbsp;to more users or talk to the right users.”</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-from-ideas-to-products">From ideas to products</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“Stripe is a place that is&nbsp;excited&nbsp;about ideas, new verticals, and ways to push the product and the company,” says Raylene Yung, who started the volunteer organization <a href="https://www.usdigitalresponse.org/">U.S. Digital Response</a> during Covid to help the government with tech talent and then worked in the Biden administration in a number of roles. The process is “very bottoms up,” adds Ryan Wang, CEO of <a href="https://www.assembled.com/">Assembled</a>, which&nbsp;uses AI to empower and scale customer support teams and who built&nbsp;the first machine learning system for Stripe&#8217;s customer support. &#8220;Nobody asked us to work on this customer support project.&#8221;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But support was becoming a big problem as the company grew. He recalls once going to the&nbsp;Collisons’ apartment&nbsp;not far from&nbsp;Stripe’s San Francisco offices to process customer requests and another time when 10 to 20 people gathered on the office&#8217;s top floor to respond to support issues. “I was new, and&nbsp;it probably took me an hour to do one ticket,” Wang recalls. “Then you see Patrick slam his laptop down. ‘Alright, I did 20,’ and he walks off to do something else.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When Jeremy Hoon (who worked at Stripe from 2013 to 2022, built its Capital lending business, and is now working on a new startup) tells me initiatives like Capital started as “small teams,”&nbsp;he’s&nbsp;not exaggerating. Typically, these new products would be teams of two or three, akin to a startup with multiple cofounders.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“A lot of things are done by very few people,” says Yung, who now sits on the board of multiple startups in the civics and climate sectors. “You might say, ‘Oh wow, there’s so many projects.&#8217; But then on the inside,&nbsp;you’d&nbsp;realize some of them are one-person, two-person [teams], so you could afford to have a lot of these projects.” Someone has to keep working on the core products, and the best way to get a spot on a hot new product team or to start your own was to be great at your current job.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This lean approach could only last so long before even the most&nbsp;crack&nbsp;team would need more support. “I was brought in with huge mandates and very little resources,” says Nunez of his role working on documentation. He had two technical writers during his first year working on internal docs, “but it wasn&#8217;t enough,” he says. “We had&nbsp;really bad&nbsp;tools. We&nbsp;didn&#8217;t&nbsp;have enough resources to build our own or make them better.&nbsp;So&nbsp;I ended up putting all the resources on the external documentation for a bit, despite my manager at the time saying,&nbsp;<em>Patrick&#8217;s not going to like this</em>” because he knew Patrick cared about internal docs.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Nunez ignored the internal doc effort as the company grew from 500 to 7,500 people. “That was when it became untenable,” he says. Nunez had compiled the evidence of how&nbsp;their&nbsp;tools&nbsp;were&nbsp;hurting productivity: New engineers were taking longer to merge their first lines of code and documents, which were spread across seven or eight&nbsp;programs, were a top complaint in a quarterly survey of developers. He got the engineers and technical writers he needed to build Trailhead, which allowed engineers to find what they needed among the company’s then half-million&nbsp;docs.&nbsp;“There was somebody who had told me early on that Patrick and John have to feel the pain themselves a lot of times in order to make a decision, and then you&#8217;ll get resources,” he says.&nbsp;“You&nbsp;won&#8217;t&nbsp;get punished for letting them feel the pain.&nbsp;You’ll&nbsp;get rewarded” with the resources you need. The Collisons, he says, &#8220;would <em>seek</em> pain,&#8221; noting that Patrick would do rotations on real teams. &nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Collisons, says Michael Manapat, who was among the first 50 employees at the company when he started in 2013 and is now CEO of <a href="https://www.rowspace.ai/">Rowspace</a>, which builds AI infrastructure for asset management firms so they can&nbsp;leverage their institutional data to make better&nbsp;decisions with AI, can&nbsp;be&nbsp;“extremely&nbsp;demanding on&nbsp;outcomes.” Yet they do so without micromanaging. “I was always impressed at how carefully they were thinking about things I was working on and how specific and granular the feedback would be,” he says. “But it was all in service of, &#8216;Now you go run this thing and make it the best version of what it could be.’”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">That freedom has helped many Stripes to become founders. But really, when you&#8217;re working at a company which wants to create more entrepreneurs and build tools to support them, the whole culture fuels that spirit. &#8220;At Stripe, you were behind the scenes looking at what was being built, but not just what was being built, but which business models and financial incentives are really working,&#8221; says Thatch’s Stevenson.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“You&#8217;re just exposed to this endless kaleidoscope of companies of all shapes and sizes that are coming out of the woodwork,” says Zo&#8217;s Guo. “Often, you&#8217;re talking directly to these teams about what they&#8217;re doing. It sparks a lot of creativity on what&#8217;s possible.”</p>
