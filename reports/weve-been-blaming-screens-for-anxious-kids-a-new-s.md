# We’ve been blaming screens for anxious kids. A new study points to a completely different culprit

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-15T09:30:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91572165/screens-anxious-kids-study-perfectionism](https://www.fastcompany.com/91572165/screens-anxious-kids-study-perfectionism)

## Summary
New research published in Psychological Bulletin finds perfectionism is rising sharply among young people, driven not by screen time but by economic inequality and declining economic mobility, with 'socially prescribed perfectionism' accelerating most dramatically since the early 2000s.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
Rising perfectionism among the incoming workforce has direct implications for organizational culture: employees who internalize impossibly high standards are more prone to burnout, fear of failure, and avoidance of experimentation—undermining psychological safety and innovation. Leaders must recognize that perfectionism is increasingly a systemic response to economic precarity, not merely an individual trait.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should design performance management systems and cultures that explicitly normalize failure and learning, counteracting the perfectionist tendencies new workforce entrants bring with them. This means investing in psychological safety initiatives, reframing mistakes as developmental rather than evaluative, and auditing whether organizational reward structures inadvertently amplify perfectionist pressures that lead to burnout and disengagement.

## Strength Score
**6/10** — While the article is primarily about a societal/psychological trend rather than an organizational case study, it has genuine relevance for organizational psychology. Rising perfectionism directly impacts workforce mental health, employee burnout, willingness to take risks, and learning cultures. The connection to workplace performance management, psychological safety, and organizational demands for ever-higher performance standards is real but requires the reader to bridge from the research to organizational application—the article itself doesn't deeply explore workplace dynamics.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">As a kid, I was a perfectionist who had <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/short-circuit-panic-with-this-simple-breathing-exercise.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">meltdowns</a> if I colored even a little outside the lines. Arguably, my unrealistic standards for myself might have helped me perform better in school. But I still wouldn’t recommend them to anyone. My perfectionism didn’t just make me cranky and hard to deal with. (Sorry, Mom.) It also often prevented me from engaging in the messy but essential process of <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/do-something-new-today-youll-be-happier-tomorrow-new-research-shows.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">trying new things</a>, screwing up, and <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/5-mindset-blocks-that-stop-you-from-learning-from-your-mistakes.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">learning from experience</a>. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I’ve always thought this was just a personal character trait (one I’ve <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/psychology-professor-explains-how-stopped-being-lazy-how-hack-personality.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">labored hard to change</a> over the years). But perhaps I was wrong. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">New research has found that perfectionism is steadily rising among young people in a way that suggests culture, not genetics, is at least partially to blame. Researchers even have a prime suspect. And for once, it isn’t screens.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-from-perfectionist-kid-to-perfectionism-researcher-nbsp">From perfectionist kid to perfectionism researcher&nbsp;</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Thomas Curran is now a professor of psychology at the London School of Economics. But he was once a young perfectionist just like me. While doing postdoctoral work in Australia, he found himself “crushed with anxiety, exhaustion, and panic attacks,”<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/society/2023/jun/04/the-rise-of-perfectionism-and-the-harm-its-doing-us-all" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank"> a <em>Guardian </em>profile</a> reports. Despite his rise from working-class roots to a promising academic career, he was convinced that he was a failure. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Sheer misery forced Curran to reassess his perfectionism. “I was genuinely of the belief that perfectionism was the one thing that was holding me up when everything around me was collapsing,” he explained. “But it was actually perfectionism that was creating those problems.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The switch in thinking gave him relief. It also gave him the research specialty&nbsp;<a href="https://amzn.to/3SwCBtX" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">he’s known for today</a>. Inspired by his own struggles, he began investigating the prevalence of perfectionism among young people as well as its psychological consequences. His latest study on the subject just came out, and it’s not good news.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-perfectionism-epidemic-is-getting-worse">The perfectionism epidemic is getting worse</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Curran&nbsp;<a href="https://www.lse.ac.uk/research/research-for-the-world/health/over-stressed-and-under-pressure-the-problem-with-being-perfect" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">previously documented</a>&nbsp;a steady increase in perfectionist tendencies among young people up to 2017. For his latest paper,&nbsp;<a href="https://www.apa.org/pubs/journals/releases/bul-bul0000518.pdf" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">published in&nbsp;<em>Psychological Bulletin</em></a>, Curran wanted to see whether perfectionism has continued to rise since. Together with a team of collaborators, he gathered data from more than 300 studies examining 82,000 college students in the U.S., U.K., and Canada between 1989 and 2024.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Perfectionism is a complex trait, involving a combination of setting crazy high standards for yourself, setting them for other people, and believing others set them for you. The study measured all three aspects of perfectionism. They’re all increasing, though not at the same rates.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“Perfectionism is rising on every dimension we measured,” <a href="https://www.psypost.org/perfectionism-is-skyrocketing-in-young-adults-and-economic-pressure-might-be-the-culprit/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Curran told <em>PsyPost</em></a>, but “the most striking part is that one form, socially prescribed perfectionism (the sense that other people demand perfection of you), is accelerating, on a curve that turned sharply upward around the early 2000s.” </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-it-s-not-the-phones-it-s-the-inequality-nbsp">It’s not the phones, it’s the inequality&nbsp;</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The timing there is interesting. <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/all-the-studies-showing-kids-these-days-are-doing-better-than-you-think/91319264" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Young people’s mental health</a> woes often get blamed on the <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/3-tips-to-give-your-kids-the-best-holiday-present-you-can-get-a-healthier-relationship-with-screens/91278115" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">spread of phones and social media</a>. But the rise in perfectionism is likely contributing. <a href="https://www.apa.org/news/press/releases/2026/05/young-adults-perfectionistic" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">As Curran commented</a>, “Perfectionism is a public health risk—it’s associated with increased depression and anxiety.” And the rapid uptick in perfectionism started well before most kids had a smartphone in their pocket. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Instead, Curran’s team thinks the culprit is economics, not technology. As part of their latest study, they also looked for a link between more perfectionism and both inequality and GDP growth.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“The economics mapped on very cleanly in so much as falling GDP per capita predicted steeper rises in perfectionistic striving [fear of failure and others’ judgment], while rising income inequality predicted steeper rises in perfectionistic concern [extremely high standards],” Curran told <em>PsyPost</em>. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-perfectionism-a-reasonable-response-to-an-unreasonable-world-nbsp">Perfectionism: A reasonable response to an unreasonable world&nbsp;</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The numbers suggest a story in which young people feel it is increasingly difficult to get ahead economically. In response, they push themselves toward unrealistic, <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/why-hustle-culture-is-back-with-a-vengeance-and-ai-is-to-blame/91245916" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">pathological levels of striving</a> and accomplishment. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Curran is a case study. He made his way into a prestigious academic career despite earning three D&#8217;s on his end-of-high-school exams. Most of his students now get straight A’s, he tells <em>The Guardian</em>. The goal posts have moved and kids have noticed. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“Perfectionism is a subconscious internalisation of a system that demands ever higher standards of those who want to participate in it,” the article sums up. <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/money-guru-ramit-sethi-the-best-time-to-give-your-kids-money-is-not-when-you-die/91343084" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Homeownership is far harder</a> than for past generations. Stable careers can feel like relics of the past. The <a href="https://www.npr.org/2026/05/28/nx-s1-5836525/affordability-report-brookings-inflation-wages" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">affordability crisis</a> only piles on the pressure. One recent survey found half of us feel <a href="https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/09/american-dream-out-of-reach-most-people-right-now-cnbc-survey.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">the American dream is out of reach for most people</a>. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“No wonder it’s messing with our heads,” the article concludes.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-time-to-go-easier-on-yourself-nbsp">Time to go easier on yourself&nbsp;</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There’s a hard lesson here and a relatively easier one. The tough truth is that rising perfectionism and <a href="https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20180219-toxic-perfectionism-is-on-the-rise" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">its steep cost to young people’s mental health</a> are probably driven by collective realities beyond the control of any one individual. You can delete <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/tiktok" id="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/tiktok" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank" type="link">TikTok</a> from your phone. It’s still not going to make buying a house in your hometown any more doable. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Building a fairer, more humane society is long, collective work. <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/suffering-outrage-fatigue-research-says-this-is-the-no-1-way-to-keep-the-news-from-stealing-your-energy-and-focus/91152076" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">We should all pitch in</a>. But it’ll be a while before that struggle makes a dent on young people’s peace of mind. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In the meantime, there is a quicker, more individual takeaway. Understanding the dynamics driving the spike in perfectionism should help young people <a href="https://www.inc.com/jessica-stillman/self-compassion-mental-health-wonder-drug-stop-procrastinating-save-money-succeed.html" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">go a little easier on themselves</a>. Unlike me obsessing about straight margins on every homework assignment in the ’90s, today’s perfectionism isn’t just internal weirdness. It’s driven by very real external realities.   </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">That doesn’t make it any less destructive. But it should make it easier to forgive yourself your anxieties and accept the very human reality that you are and will always be less than perfect.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
