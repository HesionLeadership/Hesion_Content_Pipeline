# Morning Briefing — July 16, 2026

## Top Story
**Gallup CEO says colonizing Mars may be closer than fixing today's 'broken' workplace** (Fortune). Jon Clifton's data showing global disengagement back at 2020 levels is the most consequential signal for Hesion's audience because it indicts the entire engagement industry — including many of the approaches CHROs and COOs are currently funding. This is a credibility moment: the organizations still running engagement playbooks from 2018 (survey, report, perks, repeat) now have definitive evidence that the model is broken. The real lever, as the research consistently shows, is manager quality, job design, and systemic accountability — exactly where organizational psychologists should be directing executive attention.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**The great AI layoff is turning into the great AI rehire** (Fast Company). This story has a perfect LinkedIn arc: counterintuitive reversal, named companies (IKEA, IBM) on the right side, and a clear "I told you so" moment for anyone who argued against panic-driven headcount cuts. **Suggested hook:** "Companies that fired people to fund AI are now spending more to hire them back. The ones that kept and retrained their people? They're winning. Here's what CHROs should learn from the most expensive mistake of 2025."

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**For women, being creative at work comes with a hidden cost** (Fast Company). This story deserves a deep dive because it exposes a structural contradiction most organizations haven't reckoned with: they're demanding creativity and innovation from everyone while operating within social systems that punish women for displaying exactly those behaviors. Pete can layer in role congruity theory, the psychological safety research (and its limits), and the DBS Bank story (Story 1) as a contrast case — showing how even well-designed innovation KPIs can backfire if the organization doesn't address who actually feels safe enough to take creative risks. This is a 700-word piece waiting to happen, and it positions Hesion at the intersection of inclusion and performance strategy rather than treating them as separate conversations.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Your Company Doesn't Need You as Much as You Think. That's the Problem** (Inc). This is a perfect live exercise for a leadership offsite or keynote. The concept it illustrates — founder/leader dependency as a diagnostic signal of organizational design failure — is immediately testable. Pete can pose the question to the room: "If you disappeared for two weeks tomorrow, what would break?" Then map responses to delegation gaps, role clarity failures, and pipeline deficits. It's visceral, slightly uncomfortable, and moves executives from abstraction to self-assessment in sixty seconds.
