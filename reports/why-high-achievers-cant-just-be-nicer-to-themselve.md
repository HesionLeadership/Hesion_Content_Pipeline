# Why high achievers can’t just ‘be nicer’ to themselves

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-18T05:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91572696/why-high-achievers-cant-just-be-nicer-to-themselves](https://www.fastcompany.com/91572696/why-high-achievers-cant-just-be-nicer-to-themselves)

## Summary
The article argues that traditional self-compassion advice fails high achievers because it skips the critical step of understanding why they behave as they do. It proposes 'intelligent self-compassion'—grounded in evidence, play, curiosity, and improv techniques—as a prerequisite for sustainable personal and organizational change.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The piece connects individual self-compassion deficits to organizational change failure, drawing on Kegan and Lahey's 'immunity to change' framework to explain why well-designed change initiatives don't stick when employees lack self-understanding. Leaders who don't understand their own resistance patterns inadvertently model and perpetuate rigidity throughout organizations.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should recognize that change management programs fail not because of poor design but because they're imposed on people who haven't developed self-understanding. Building routines of reflective practice, curiosity-driven inquiry, and psychological safety for leaders to examine their own resistance patterns is a prerequisite for sustainable culture change—not an afterthought.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If you&#8217;re a high achiever, you&#8217;ve probably been told—in various formats and with varying degrees of sincerity—to be kinder to yourself. Choose kindness. You are enough.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For most, that advice likely lands somewhere between mildly irritating and completely useless.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This has nothing to do with who you are. It&#8217;s a problem with how mainstream self-compassion is being taught without considering the state or pace of the world. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The dominant narrative assumes that self-compassion is a feeling you can simply decide to access, a kind of internal tone adjustment. For people who have built their careers (and identities) on performance, output, and the relentless pursuit of success, that framing doesn&#8217;t just fail to connect, it slides off like teflon. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">You cannot willpower your way into self-compassion.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-many-change-efforts-start-in-the-wrong-place">Why many change efforts start in the wrong place</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Intelligent self-compassion matters far beyond individual well-being. There&#8217;s a direct line from self-understanding to organizational effectiveness that most change management frameworks have never considered. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When people don&#8217;t understand why they behave the way they do, they interpret their own patterns as evidence of personal failure. That judgement of the self generates resistance; not just metaphorical resistance, but the neurological, structural kind that Robert Kegan and Lisa Lahey, developmental psychologists at Harvard and coauthors, describe as &#8220;immunity to change.&#8221; </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Their immunity to change work confirms that humans have a fundamental drive to both evolve and survive simultaneously. We are putting our foot on the accelerator and brake at the same time. Result: a kind of psychological tug-of-war that can make change feel insurmountable. This survival drive makes us immune to psychological threats like failure and being seen as incompetent by our peers. That resistance prevents change. And then people judge themselves harder for not changing.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most change efforts attempt to shift behavior in people who don&#8217;t have self-compassion, because they lack understanding. It&#8217;s why well-designed initiatives, training programs, and culture change efforts so often fail to stick despite genuine commitment from everyone involved. The change is imposed on people who are not prepared; it’s imposed on people who don’t yet understand. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Gartner’s <a href="https://www.gartner.com/en/articles/adopting-change-in-the-workplace">2026 report </a>on trends affecting HR officers states that inspiring change isn’t the answer and that establishing it as a routine is three times as effective than the inspirational approach. This is change not as a “feel-good vibe” but as a routine. And change can only become routine when people fundamentally understand their own resistance to it—starting with leaders and their own relationship/resistance to it. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-compassion-requires-comprehension">Compassion requires comprehension</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">You cannot be genuinely compassionate toward something you don&#8217;t understand. This is true of other people. We’re naturally warm towards others and want to support them when we understand their situation. The same logic applies internally, but the self-compassion conversation has largely skipped over it.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Kristin Neff&#8217;s foundational <a href="https://greatergood.berkeley.edu/images/uploads/Neff_Handouts_(1).pdf">research</a> at the University of Texas at Austin identifies three components of self-compassion: self-kindness, common humanity and mindfulness. This is important work, but the framework assumes you can access self-kindness independently of understanding why you behave the way you do. Many high achievers would find mindfulness inaccessible when their nervous system is fried. Just because we can find self-compassion for others does not automatically mean we can do the same for ourselves. For high achievers especially, that&#8217;s precisely where it breaks down. You can&#8217;t be kind to something and want to take action towards change if you&#8217;re still prosecuting it.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">What&#8217;s needed first is <em>intelligent self-compassion</em>: self-compassion grounded in evidence rather than willpower. This is not a feeling you manufacture, but a well-researched conclusion you reach.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">With most high achievers being cerebral, live-in-your-head types, the idea of “data or it didn’t happen” takes on new meaning. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Consider what happens when a high achiever finally gets a clinical explanation for a pattern they&#8217;ve been managing through sheer force for years.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Take ADHD as an example. This is a condition that’s both underdiagnosed and frequently misdiagnosed in high-performing adults, partly because many have developed sophisticated compensatory strategies in order to mask and function in society. Speaking from my own experience with a late diagnosis at 39, one of the masking strategies I’ve used—one that shows up especially in women—is high achievement. For someone who has spent years interpreting distraction, hyperfocus and all-or-nothing thinking as personal failures, receiving a clinical explanation isn&#8217;t just informative. It&#8217;s liberating. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When you understand how your brain works, how your conditions shape your behavior, and how achievement culture has exploited your natural drive to perform and belong, self-blame stops being logical. It&#8217;s not forgiveness because forgiveness implies there was something to be guilty of or wrong in the first place. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-self-compassion-through-play-curiosity-and-improv">Self-compassion through play, curiosity, and improv</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Understanding removes the<em> </em>grounds for self-judgement; it doesn&#8217;t automatically dissolve its grip. For that, something different is required. The good news is, these are not skills you need to learn, but instincts you need to remember. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The first is play—not as recreation, but as a cognitive state or mode of operating. A leading play researcher, Dr. Stuart Brown of The National Institute for Play, has done <a href="https://www.fa-sett.no/filer/Consequences_of_Play_Deprivation-Stuart_Brown_MD.pdf">research</a> showing that <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/91548185/your-workforce-doesnt-need-more-ai-it-needs-play" id="https://www.fastcompany.com/91548185/your-workforce-doesnt-need-more-ai-it-needs-play" type="link">play deprivation makes belief systems rigid and fixed</a>. Erik Erikson, an expert in human growth and development, defined play as a situation in which a child can work through experiences by creating model situations and master reality through planning and experimentation. Marc Bekoff, an American ecologist and evolutionary biologist, describes <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/11931002_Mammalian_Play_Training_for_the_Unexpected">play as training for the unexpected</a>. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When we&#8217;re genuinely in a playful state, the tight hold we have on our beliefs, ideas, and assumptions loosens. What most people fail to realize is that their assumptions, about themselves and the world, are subconsciously dictating their behavior, including the assumption “I am not enough.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The second is <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/91536344/how-ai-inhibits-our-curiosity-and-what-to-do-to-regain-it-according-to-science" id="https://www.fastcompany.com/91536344/how-ai-inhibits-our-curiosity-and-what-to-do-to-regain-it-according-to-science" type="link">curiosity</a>—specifically, replacing the judgmental internal voice with an investigative one. The shift from &#8220;what&#8217;s wrong with me&#8221; to &#8220;isn&#8217;t that interesting&#8221; is not semantic. It&#8217;s a neurologically distinct state, one of genuine inquiry as opposed to judging. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Together, these two capabilities do what understanding alone can&#8217;t: They make it possible to hold the self-judgement lightly enough that it loosens its grip.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A third tool worth naming, borrowed from applied improvisation, is &#8220;Yes, and.&#8221; This is the concept of acknowledging the reality of a situation, not necessarily agreeing with it, and then creating a path forward. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It sounds simple, because it is, and it&#8217;s one of the fastest routes to intelligent self-compassion available because it refuses to let one feeling or one version of a person be the whole story.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">High achievers are particularly susceptible to binary self-assessment. The missed deadline cancels out the successful quarter or the anxious spiral from some feedback disproves the competence. This either/or framing doesn&#8217;t just fail to describe human experience accurately, it produces suffering, because every contradictory feeling becomes evidence of failure. The human experience is not so black and white—it consists of a multitude of emotions. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">&#8220;Yes, and&#8221; dissolves the binary. <em>Yes, I struggled today. And I have not always struggled. And I won&#8217;t always struggle. And I&#8217;ll likely struggle again.</em> It&#8217;s the full timeline of a person, not a verdict on one data point.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-accept-that-you-were-never-broken-in-the-first-place">Accept that you were never broken in the first place</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Self-compassion isn&#8217;t about forgiveness or good vibes. Forgiveness implies a fault and good vibes quickly evaporate. Rather, it&#8217;s a conclusion you reach once the evidence is realized: You were never broken in the first place.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But like any conclusion worth reaching, it doesn’t stick as a one-off insight. The diagnosis, reframe, and moment of &#8220;isn&#8217;t that interesting&#8221; are starting points of a journey, not a destination. Intelligent self-compassion becomes real the way anything becomes real—through repetition. Understanding the pattern, loosening the grip, holding multiple truths, and then doing it again tomorrow is a practice that makes room for the investigative voice to answer before the prosecuting one does.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">High achievers need what they&#8217;ve always trusted—evidence.</p>
