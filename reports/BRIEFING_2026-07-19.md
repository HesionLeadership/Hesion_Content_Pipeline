# Morning Briefing — July 19, 2026

## Top Story
**Gallup CEO says colonizing Mars may be closer than fixing today's 'broken' workplace** (Fortune). Four in five workers globally are disengaged—right back to 2020 levels—despite billions spent on engagement initiatives over the past decade. This is a damning indictment of how most organizations approach engagement and a direct opening for Hesion's message: surface-level programs without systemic redesign of management quality, autonomy, and meaning are fundamentally insufficient. Pair this with the Inc story showing 50% of employees are now actively looking to leave, and you have a ticking time bomb for any CHRO who mistook a frozen labor market for a healthy culture.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**Your company doesn't have a talent shortage. It has a talent visibility problem** (Fast Company). The 21-point gap between managers who think they know their people's skills (90%) and employees who agree (69%) is a perfect, specific, counterintuitive data point that stops the scroll. **Suggested hook:** *"90% of managers say they understand their team's skills. Only 69% of employees agree. That 21-point gap is costing you more than any recruiter."*

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**Women and men in the C-suite see 2 different workplaces** (Fast Company) + **What 60 Years of Data Reveals About How Men and Women Experience Leadership** (HBR). These are essentially two takes on the same landmark 60-year HBR dataset and together they deserve a deep-dive newsletter. The org psych angle is rich: perceptual asymmetry, "prove-it-again" bias, organizational sensemaking failure, and the critical distinction between formal policy equality and experienced equality. Pete can unpack why representation metrics alone are misleading, introduce the concept of perception audits segmented by demographic, and offer a concrete framework for CHROs to diagnose and close the gap between what leadership *thinks* the culture feels like and what different groups *actually* experience. This is a 700-word piece that positions Hesion as thinking beyond surface DEI metrics.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Stripe is the most underrated—and influential—$159 billion company in the world** (Fast Company). This is a perfect case study for illustrating the tension between psychological safety and high performance standards—a concept Pete likely teaches frequently. The specifics are unusually concrete: rigorous writing practices that reduce political decision-making, leaders who do operational work rather than relying on filtered reports, "altitude shifting" as a hiring criterion, and a culture that produced 268 alumni founders. It directly counters the false binary that organizations must choose between being demanding and being safe. Use it to illustrate: **"High performance culture isn't about pressure. It's about cooperative rigor."**
