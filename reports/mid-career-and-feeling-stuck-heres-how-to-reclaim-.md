# Mid-career and feeling stuck? Here’s how to reclaim your value

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-17T05:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91572679/mid-career-and-feeling-stuck-heres-how-to-reclaim-your-value](https://www.fastcompany.com/91572679/mid-career-and-feeling-stuck-heres-how-to-reclaim-your-value)

## Summary
Mid-career professionals are absorbing increasing workloads as organizations flatten management layers, yet receiving no corresponding increases in title, pay, or recognition. The article offers a three-part framework for mapping role creep, articulating business value, and renegotiating scope and recognition.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
Organizational delayering creates a structural equity gap where mid-level employees perform higher-level work without commensurate rewards, eroding perceived organizational justice and psychological ownership. This mirrors research on effort-reward imbalance and its links to disengagement and burnout.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should proactively audit how flattening redistributes work and decision-making authority to mid-level roles, then formalize recognition, scope adjustments, and compensation to match the actual work being performed—before silent overload drives attrition of the organization's most critical execution layer.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Skim the headlines and today&#8217;s career story belongs to two groups: anxious new grads at the bottom and embattled executives at the top. U.S. entry-level postings have fallen about 35% since early 2023, according to labor research firm <a href="https://www.reveliolabs.com/news/macro/is-ai-responsible-for-the-rise-in-entry-level-unemployment/">Revelio Labs</a>. Above them, the structure is thinning: in <a href="https://www.kornferry.com/insights/featured-topics/workforce-management-articles/workforce-planning-insights">a 2025 Korn Ferry survey</a> of 15,000 professionals, 41% said their companies trimmed management layers last year.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Far less gets said about the band in between, where most people build their careers.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This is the layer of experienced individual contributors, managers, and directors who execute company strategy. Some of these roles get cut too. But when a layer thins, the work it held doesn’t disappear. It settles onto whoever remains, and most of it lands in the middle.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Their roles may feel stable, but the work keeps growing in scope and difficulty while the title and pay stay put. In practice, they’re doing more senior work without the promotion or raise that would normally come with it. Simply put, they begin to lose agency long before they lose the job.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The countermove is precision about what should belong to you, and the value you want to be known for.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Here is a three-part exercise to help today’s mid-careerists carve out a path to growth:</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-1-map-what-s-changed-in-your-role">1. Map what’s changed in your role</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most mid-career professionals carry the same assumption: If I were ready for the next level, someone would have promoted me by now. That logic only works if the ladder above you is intact. In a compressed organization, the better question becomes: What changed around me that no one bothered to map out?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Start by listing the core work that has always been yours. This gives you a baseline for what the role was <em>meant</em> to be before the org started changing it.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Sort your current work into three to four columns:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Came from below</strong>: work once done by junior staff, assistants, or vendors</li>



<li><strong>Came from above</strong>: work your old manager or leadership used to own</li>



<li><strong>Came from the side</strong>: coordination across teams, projects, or tools, none of which were yours to begin with&nbsp;</li>



<li><strong>Came from the systems: </strong>managing, monitoring, or “owning” new <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence" title="AI">AI</a> tools and workflows</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Circle the work where your judgment changes the outcome, the work that gets worse, slower, or riskier if anyone else does it. That is your keep pile. Underline the work that doesn&#8217;t need you specifically, and next to each underlined item, write its exit: automate, delegate, redesign, or drop.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-2-translate-the-keep-pile-into-business-value">2. Translate the keep-pile into business value</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Review your circled items and ask: Which decisions are made more effectively because I&#8217;m in the room? What systems do I keep from breaking?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Then write one sentence for the role you play now, in a fixed shape: I [do what] so that [business result]. For example, “I turn AI outputs into customer-ready strategy.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Back it up with outcomes. Start each line with what changed, and where you can, by how much. A quick test: If a line could open with &#8220;I helped,&#8221; rewrite it until it opens with the result. Reduced launch delays by forcing tradeoffs early. Held the team&#8217;s output steady as the layer above it thinned.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-3-renegotiate-around-the-role-you-play-now">3. Renegotiate around the role you play now</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Now it’s time to answer an important question: Given the role I am already performing, what needs to change in my scope, support, and recognition?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Bring the results of this exercise to your manager, ideally ahead of a planning or review cycle.&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>First, name what changed: &#8220;Over the last year I&#8217;ve taken on [a, b, c] that used to sit with [a junior role or another team].&#8221;&nbsp;</li>



<li>Then, name the value: &#8220;Here&#8217;s the role I&#8217;m effectively playing now, and here&#8217;s what it&#8217;s producing,&#8221; using your one sentence and your outcomes.&nbsp;</li>



<li>Finally, make a two-part ask: &#8220;I&#8217;d like to shift toward [the judgment-heavy work] and away from [one or two low-value buckets]. What can we automate, delegate, or drop so I&#8217;m not carrying both? And given the scope this role has taken on, I&#8217;d like to talk about recognition, whether that&#8217;s decision rights, title, compensation, or visibility with [specific leaders].&#8221;</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Raising this during cuts can feel like volunteering for scrutiny. But companies that flatten cut what they cannot see; naming your work keeps it visible. Your manager may not grant all of it. The conversation still reframes overload as structural rather than personal, and forces decisions about what you&#8217;ll keep absorbing in silence.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Mid-career used to be the holding pattern between paying your dues and getting the big title. Now it is the pressure point and the leverage point at once. You do not control how your company compresses. But you do control whether your job keeps expanding toward more, or toward the right things.</p>
