# The robot byline is quietly disappearing

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-23T08:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91575942/ai-robot-byline-quietly-disappearing](https://www.fastcompany.com/91575942/ai-robot-byline-quietly-disappearing)

## Summary
News organizations are quietly abandoning AI bylines on machine-generated content due to trust penalties, search ranking disadvantages, and reputational risk, even as AI-assisted writing increases across newsrooms.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The story highlights how accountability structures break down when authorship is diffused to machines—paralleling organizational research on diffusion of responsibility. The Sacramento Bee reporters' pushback illustrates how professional identity and psychological ownership over work products drive resistance to having one's name attached to AI-generated output without consent.

## Leadership Lesson
When integrating AI tools into knowledge work, leaders must preserve clear human accountability chains and respect workers' psychological ownership of their professional output. Policies should scale disclosure with AI contribution level, and employees should never be forced to attach their professional reputation to work they didn't meaningfully control—doing so erodes trust, identity, and quality standards.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The most obvious use of generative <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence" title="AI">AI</a> is writing. It&#8217;s right there in the name—large language models (LLMs) are all about reading, organizing, analyzing, and conjuring words—which is exactly why many in the journalism profession have been going through a kind of existential crisis these past few years.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And the crisis isn&#8217;t just theoretical. As artificial intelligence systems get better at writing, a growing number of newsrooms are using AI to help not just with analysis, process, and ideas, but the actual words, too. That&#8217;s leading to growing pushback from editorial teams, like when reporters at <em>The Sacramento Bee</em> <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/01/business/media/mcclatchy-ai-newsroom-byline-strike.html">recently objected</a> to having their bylines put on content written primarily by AI.</p>





<p class="wp-block-paragraph">As I teach in my <a href="https://mediacopilot.ai/ai-courses/">AI trainings</a>, using AI to write public-facing content is an inherently dicey business. I should know, since AI-generated articles are a component of <em>The Media Copilot</em>&#8216;s editorial strategy. At minimum, you need to ask yourself several ethical questions: What is the medium? What exactly was the AI&#8217;s role? What&#8217;s the worst that could happen? And several more. From the answers, you need to develop a clear rulebook.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">One of the most important parts of such a rulebook is its policy around disclosure—the ways you signal to the reader that a piece of content is AI-generated or AI-assisted. The most direct way to do so is with an AI byline. Robot bylines typically have clinical names, such as the <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/thread/345442481/what-is-google-e-e-a-t?hl=en">AI News Desk</a> or <a href="https://www.espnfrontrow.com/2024/09/enhancing-espns-game-recaps-for-underserved-sports-using-ai/">Generative AI Services</a>, along with their own author pages. That unambiguously lets the reader know that AI didn&#8217;t help just with research or ideas, but also with the words on the page. To what extent—which is often crucial—is usually revealed via an accompanying disclaimer, often at the bottom of the page.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-transparency-comes-with-a-cost">Transparency comes with a cost</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The AI byline feels intuitively correct: It checks the transparency box, it&#8217;s an easy-to-read label, and it slots neatly into an existing system. Moreover, audiences say they want them, with a <a href="https://trustingnews.org/new-research-how-ai-disclosures-in-news-help-and-also-hurt-trust-with-audiences/">Trusting News study</a> showing that 94% of readers want disclosures on content that&#8217;s AI-written. As use of AI rises in newsrooms and editorial operations, it seems logical that AI bylines will become more common.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Except that&#8217;s not what&#8217;s happening. <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.18774">A 2025 audit</a> of 186,000 articles in 1,500 newspapers in the U.S. estimated that about 9% of the content was partly or fully AI-generated, yet only about 5% of those articles included a disclosure. Prominent AI-byline experiments at <a href="https://fortune.com/author/fortune-intelligence/"><em>Fortune</em></a> and <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/author/business-insider-ai-news-desk"><em>Business Insider</em></a> were discontinued, though that&#8217;s not an indicator of a broad policy shift against AI writing; indeed, Nick Lichtenberg, business editor at <em>Fortune</em>, <a href="https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/news/viral-profile-AI-anxieties-Nick-Lichtenberg-fortune">famously used AI</a> to produce more than 600 stories in six months. The Cleveland <em>Plain Dealer</em>—which <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/91495946/ai-can-write-now-what-happens-reporters">uses writing tools to turn raw reporting into stories</a> with final sign-off from the reporter—generally doesn&#8217;t use an AI byline unless the human contribution is extremely minimal.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Anecdotally, as someone who covers AI use in media closely, I see fewer AI bylines than I used to. Which isn&#8217;t to say they&#8217;re gone: CoinDesk marks AI assistance with the byline <a href="https://www.coindesk.com/author/ai-boost">&#8220;AI Boost&#8221;</a> and <a href="https://mediacopilot.substack.com/p/espn-ai-articles-journalism?utm_source=publication-search">ESPN&#8217;s writing bots</a> still get top billing, but generally there&#8217;s been a retreat from the AI byline as a best practice.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">What&#8217;s going on? A few things:</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">1. <strong>Visibility in search and AI answers.</strong> Google says it does not downgrade content simply because AI was used to produce it. But its <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/thread/345442481/what-is-google-e-e-a-t?hl=en">guidance</a> consistently stresses clear authorship, first-hand expertise, and accountability, and it has said that giving AI an author byline is probably not the best way to disclose its use. A robot byline may not be a direct negative ranking signal, but it also provides none of the human authority signals that search and discovery systems are designed to reward.</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list"></ol>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Data supports this: In <a href="https://graphite.io/five-percent/ai-content-in-search-and-llms">Graphite’s 2025 analysis</a>, human-written articles made up 86% of the pages ranking in Google Search and tended to rank higher than AI-generated material. That doesn&#8217;t prove that AI authorship or attribution caused the difference, but it shows the broader disadvantage AI-heavy content faces. That pattern also matches our limited experience at <em>The Media Copilot</em>. Our human-bylined articles show up in Google Discover and rank higher in Google Search than those published under our AI byline, <em><a href="https://mediacopilot.ai/author/thecopilot/">The Copilot</a>.</em></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">2. <strong>The trust paradox.</strong> Trusting News found that, although the vast majority of audiences want AI disclosures, their presence made 42% of respondents less likely to trust an article. Separate research from Reuters Institute found an even wider comfort gap: 12% of readers are comfortable with fully AI news, versus 62% comfortable with fully human-written news.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">3. <strong>Institutional memory.</strong> When generative AI was new, there were <a href="https://mediacopilot.substack.com/p/sports-illustrated-ai-content-scandal?utm_source=publication-search">several</a> <a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/nba/comments/16iprsw/microsoft_publishes_gibberish_aigenerated/">high-profile</a> <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/media/2023/01/17/cnet-ai-articles-journalism-corrections/?utm_source=instagram&amp;utm_medium=social&amp;utm_campaign=wp_main&amp;crl8_id=9516f473-88fe-422c-95ae-bb4c5e4f0a20">failures</a> of AI content. By putting forward an AI byline, an outlet can&#8217;t avoid association with those mistakes, and it paints a target on every article under it that the screenshot industrial complex will fire at all day the moment there&#8217;s an error.</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list" start="2"></ol>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In short, AI bylines have acquired a lot of baggage in the short time they&#8217;ve been around, and many publications seem to be taking the stance that they&#8217;re just not worth it. However, that doesn&#8217;t translate into a pullback on AI generally, or even a pullback on AI-assisted content. The most direct way to square this circle is to reserve bylines for people, and disclose the AI&#8217;s contribution in some other way, usually via one of those notes at the end of the article.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-byline-is-a-promise">A byline is a promise</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">At the heart of this issue is what the byline actually means. The idea that the person named at the top of the article wrote each and every word has always been a fallacy. Editors, wire copy, fact checkers, headline writers, and spellcheckers all contribute actual words and sometimes whole passages to articles. Automated editing software takes this even further—anything substantially edited through Grammarly or a similar tool already contains wording shaped by AI.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The byline is not &#8220;I wrote all these words&#8221;; it&#8217;s &#8220;I stand by all these words.&#8221; And this is the core problem with AI bylines—they obfuscate responsibility. Without ownership, without someone prominently standing by what&#8217;s actually written, there&#8217;s little incentive to make sure it&#8217;s great writing. It inherently turns the writing into something second class, regardless of how the AI was used and how many words it actually contributed.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">At the same time, what happened at <em>The Sacramento Bee</em> shows just how much writers will defend the use of their names. And rightly so. The path forward isn&#8217;t to force writers&#8217; names onto AI content without their consent, but to give them the freedom and the accountability on the use of AI. The disclosure should scale with the machine’s contribution: Routine editing may require nothing, substantial drafting should require a specific note, and largely automated reporting should explain both the system and the human review. Ultimately, though, the only label that really matters is whether a named human stands behind the result. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">With the right policies and training, publications can give writers broad freedom to use AI while keeping responsibility attached to a human name. If the content is worthwhile, then over time those who use AI to amplify and accelerate their judgment will be successful. Those who use it as a substitute for thinking will inevitably fail.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-human-accountability-signal">The human accountability signal</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The AI byline isn&#8217;t dead, but it is being repriced. As machine text gets cheap, a human name that carries real accountability becomes the scarce and valuable signal. As the tools get better, they&#8217;ll continue to blur who wrote what. The constant, however, is simple: A human has to stand behind it. No one gets to outsource accountability to a machine.</p>
