# Morning Briefing — July 18, 2026

## Top Story
**Gallup CEO says colonizing Mars may be closer than fixing today's 'broken' workplace** (Fortune). Four in five workers globally are disengaged — back to 2020 levels — despite billions spent on engagement programs over the past decade. This is the single most damning indictment of how organizations currently approach engagement, and it's a direct conversation-starter with every CHRO and COO Pete advises: whatever your organization is doing right now is almost certainly not working, and the data says it's time to stop optimizing broken approaches and fundamentally rethink how you design work, develop managers, and create accountability for engagement outcomes. Pair this with the "job-hugging is cracking" story below and you have a ticking retention bomb.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**Your company doesn't have a talent shortage. It has a talent visibility problem** (Fast Company). The 21-point gap between managers who think they know their people's skills (90%) and employees who agree (69%) is the kind of specific, counterintuitive stat that stops the scroll. **Suggested hook:** *"90% of managers say they understand their team's skills. Only 69% of their employees agree. That 21-point gap is costing you more than any recruiter."* This frames a familiar complaint (talent shortage) as a leadership design failure — exactly Pete's lane — and invites CHROs and COOs to see themselves in the problem without being preachy.

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**Women and men in the C-suite see two different workplaces** (Fast Company) + **What 60 years of data reveals about how men and women experience leadership** (HBR). These are essentially two angles on the same 60-year longitudinal HBR study and deserve a combined deep analysis. The newsletter angle: *organizational sensemaking breaks down when leaders in the same company are experiencing fundamentally different realities.* Pete can unpack why perception audits segmented by demographics are more diagnostic than representation dashboards, why men consistently underestimate barriers (and what that means for how equity interventions land), and why "prove-it-again" bias compounds at the executive level in ways that formal policy can't touch. This has the intellectual depth for 500+ words and positions Hesion at the intersection of evidence-based practice and lived organizational reality — not culture war territory, but systems design territory.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Stripe's culture of urgency without aggression** (Fast Company). This is a perfect real-world case study for Pete's keynote on high-performance culture design. It illustrates a concept most leadership teams get wrong: you don't have to choose between psychological safety and demanding standards. Specific, teachable mechanisms — rigorous writing replacing political meetings, leaders doing operational work alongside teams rather than directing from above, "altitude shifting" as a hiring criterion, mission framing that expands talent attraction — make this immediately actionable, not just inspirational. The 268-alumni-founder stat is a knockout proof point: the culture was so generative it produced an entire ecosystem of companies.

Worth filing for any client conversation about embedding strategic priorities into performance management rather than relying on cultural exhortation.
