# Delivering on time is harder than companies admit

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-14T12:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91572035/delivering-on-time-is-harder-than-companies-admit](https://www.fastcompany.com/91572035/delivering-on-time-is-harder-than-companies-admit)

## Summary
Denis Danov, CTO of Dreamix, argues that on-time delivery is one of the hardest and least examined aspects of client trust in software services, with industry data showing only 34-59% of projects meet their deadlines, and that genuine ownership—not mandated accountability—is the key differentiator.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The article highlights the distinction between mandated accountability and intrinsic psychological ownership, arguing that genuine ownership over outcomes cannot be installed through policy but emerges from team culture, escalation norms, and individual felt responsibility. This aligns with research on psychological ownership and autonomous motivation as drivers of discretionary effort.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should recognize that reliable delivery consistency is a cultural outcome, not a process outcome—it requires building environments where employees feel genuine psychological ownership over commitments, which means investing in early honest conversations about timelines, transparent risk escalation norms, and recognition systems that celebrate reliability rather than only innovation.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I&#8217;m genuinely proud when we commit to something at the start of the month and deliver by the deadline.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">People take on-time delivery for granted. In most client relationships, on-time delivery is treated as a baseline expectation or the floor rather than the ceiling. What I&#8217;ve found after years of running software delivery is that it&#8217;s one of the hardest things to do consistently, and one of the least examined aspects of client trust.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><a href="https://wellingtone.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/The-State-of-PM.pdf">Only 34%</a> of organizations manage to deliver projects on time, according to 2024 research by Wellingtone, a UK-based project management consultancy. Two out of every three projects, across industries and company sizes, miss their deadline. This pattern, however, is not a European quirk. PMI&#8217;s 2025 Pulse of the Profession report found that <a href="https://www.pmi.org/-/media/pmi/documents/public/pdf/learning/thought-leadership/pulse/pulse_of_the_profession_2025-1.pdf">only 59%</a> of organizations kept projects on their original schedule last year.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-ownership-can-t-be-manufactured"><strong>Ownership can’t be manufactured</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There are always surprises when a team is developing software. Unexpected complexity surfaces mid-build. A dependency shifts. A requirement that seemed clear turns out not to be. An engineer gets pulled into something urgent. None of this is unusual; it&#8217;s the standard operating environment of software development. A team either absorbs those disruptions and lands where it said it would, or it doesn&#8217;t. That capacity comes from how a team is built, how problems get escalated, and whether the people doing the work feel genuine ownership over the outcome.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Ownership is the part that can’t be manufactured. You can require it, but you can&#8217;t install it. It shows whether someone stays an extra hour to fix something that falls outside their immediate responsibility, or flags a risk three weeks before it becomes a crisis.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In services companies, on-time delivery carries a specific weight. The product is the outcome we committed to producing for someone who trusted us with their budget, their roadmap, and their internal credibility. When we miss a deadline, a project metric is the least of what slips. The relationship damage takes far longer to recover from than most teams anticipate.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-unglamorous-work-of-trust"><strong>The unglamorous work of trust</strong><strong></strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Consistent delivery also accumulates in ways that don&#8217;t show up in project reports. Clients plan around it. They extend engagements based on it. They refer other clients because of it. The awards and certifications that companies tend to publicize rarely drive that kind of trust. A track record of landing on time does.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The practical side of this, however, is unglamorous. It means having honest conversations early when a timeline looks unrealistic, rather than hoping the team finds a way through. It means tracking the internal metrics that tell you whether a team is on track before the deadline arrives, not after. It also means treating a commitment to a client the way you&#8217;d treat any commitment with real consequences: The date doesn&#8217;t move because something inconvenient came up.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-final-thoughts"><strong>Final thoughts</strong><strong></strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">At Dreamix, our due date is the metric I hold onto the most. Client referrals, team morale, the ability to take on more complex work, all of it compounds when you build a reputation for delivering when you said you would.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The industry doesn&#8217;t celebrate delivery consistency the way it celebrates innovation. There are no awards for the team that shipped on time, every time, for three years.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">That gap in recognition doesn&#8217;t mean it&#8217;s not the harder achievement.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><em>Denis Danov is CTO of Dreamix Ltd. </em><em></em></p>
