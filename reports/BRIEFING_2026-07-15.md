# Morning Briefing — July 15, 2026

## Top Story
**Gallup CEO says colonizing Mars may be closer than fixing today's 'broken' workplace** (Fortune) — Jon Clifton's headline is provocative, but the data underneath is damning: four in five workers globally are disengaged, right back to 2020 levels despite billions spent on engagement initiatives. This is the single most important data point for every CHRO and COO Pete talks to because it indicts the status quo — whatever organizations are doing isn't working, and it's not a marginal miss, it's a systemic failure. This creates an urgent opening for Hesion's message: the problem isn't engagement programs, it's leadership capability and job design at the structural level.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**The great AI layoff is turning into the great AI rehire** (Fast Company) — This is a "told you so" story with receipts: companies that cut people for AI are now paying more to bring them back, while IKEA and IBM's retain-and-retrain strategies are outperforming. It's concrete, counterintuitive to the hype cycle, and positions Pete as a voice of strategic patience.

**Suggested hook:** "The most expensive decision in your 2025 workforce plan wasn't the AI investment. It was the layoffs that came with it."

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**Want experts in 10 years? Keep AI away from your beginners today** (Fast Company) — This has the deepest org psych vein to mine. Pete can connect deliberate practice theory (Ericsson), skill acquisition stages (Dreyfus model), and the concept of organizational skill atrophy into a longer argument about why CHROs need to think in 10-year talent pipeline horizons, not quarterly efficiency metrics. The Norway schools example and the Barclay Damon law firm policy give two very different but complementary case studies. The tension between short-term productivity and long-term capability development is exactly the kind of strategic tradeoff Pete's audience struggles with daily — and almost no one is writing about it with this level of specificity.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Leaders at All Levels: How DBS Bank Makes Everyone an Innovator** (MIT Sloan Review) — DBS making innovation worth 20% of every performance review is a clean, specific, replicable example Pete can use to illustrate a core principle: *what gets measured gets behavior, and what gets rewarded gets culture*. It's a perfect slide in any talk about aligning performance systems with strategic priorities. The concept it illustrates: expectancy theory in action — and the gap between cultural rhetoric ("we value innovation") and structural commitment (putting it in the scorecard). Pete can ask any audience: "What percentage of your performance review is tied to your stated strategic priorities?" and watch the room go quiet.
