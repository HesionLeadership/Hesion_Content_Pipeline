# Morning Briefing — July 22, 2026

## Top Story
**First AI takes the calls. Then your company stops listening.** This is the most urgent story for CHROs and COOs right now because it reframes the AI automation conversation from a cost play to an organizational intelligence risk. Most leadership teams are evaluating AI deployment through headcount reduction; this piece exposes what's actually being destroyed—the informal sensing networks that frontline workers provide, which no dashboard or analytics tool currently replicates. If your clients are in the middle of AI-driven workforce restructuring (and most are), this is the story that should change how they scope those projects.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**Your company doesn't have a talent shortage. It has a talent visibility problem.** This one is LinkedIn gold because it takes a universally felt frustration (the "talent war") and flips it with a specific, counterintuitive data point: 90% of managers think they know their people's skills, but only 69% of employees agree. That gap is immediately recognizable and shareable.

**Hook:** "Your managers think they know what their people can do. Your people disagree. That 21-point perception gap is costing you more than any recruiter ever will."

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**Women and men in the C-suite see 2 different workplaces** (paired with the HBR 60-year longitudinal data piece, which covers the same study from a different angle). This deserves 500+ words because the data is extraordinary—60 years of longitudinal evidence showing that the perception gap between men and women in leadership isn't closing, it's widening on key dimensions. Pete can unpack the organizational sensemaking failure at the core: leaders sitting in the same executive meetings, working under the same policies, are experiencing fundamentally different organizations. That's not a diversity problem—it's a leadership epistemology problem. There's room to connect this to practical interventions like perception audits, disaggregated engagement data, and redesigning promotion criteria with transparency. The newsletter can also address why well-intentioned male leaders consistently underestimate barriers, and what CHROs can do about that specific blind spot.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Stripe's culture of urgency without aggression.** This is a perfect real-world case study for any presentation on building high-performance cultures that don't burn people out. The specific mechanisms are teachable: writing as a decision-making discipline, leaders doing frontline work to maintain operational empathy, "altitude shifting" as a hiring criterion, and mission framing that expands the talent aperture. The 268 alumni founders stat is a powerful proof point—when your culture is healthy enough that people leave to build their own companies, you've built something generative, not extractive. Use this to illustrate the concept that psychological safety and high standards are not in tension—they're mutually reinforcing.

## Stories Worth Watching
- **The invisible labor of likability at work** — Strong org psych foundations on emotional labor and OCBs. Useful for any client conversation about performance review redesign or equitable workload distribution.
- **For women, being creative at work comes with a hidden cost** — Pairs well with the C-suite perception gap stories. The double bind between innovation demands and gender role expectations is a sharp insight for any client pushing "innovation culture" without examining who actually gets rewarded for it.
- **The Best Teams Know When to Work Alone (HBR)** — Solid, evidence-based challenge to the collaboration-as-default assumption. Good fodder for a quick social post or a slide in a hybrid work design conversation.
- **Leaders at All Levels: How DBS Bank Makes Everyone an Innovator** — The 20% innovation KPI is a concrete, citable example for any client asking "how do we actually operationalize innovation beyond slogans?"

## Skip These

The "take two weeks off" diagnostic is a useful provocation but doesn't offer enough depth or novelty for Pete's audience of CHROs and COOs, who are already past this conversation.
