# Your company doesn’t have a talent shortage. It has a talent visibility problem

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-18T05:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91574281/your-company-doesnt-have-a-talent-shortage-it-has-a-talent-visibility-problem](https://www.fastcompany.com/91574281/your-company-doesnt-have-a-talent-shortage-it-has-a-talent-visibility-problem)

## Summary
Organizations suffer from a talent visibility problem rather than a talent shortage, with a significant perception gap between managers who believe they understand their teams' skills (90%) and employees who agree (69%), leading to stalled careers, unnecessary external hiring, and preventable turnover.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The 21-point perception gap between managers and employees on skills awareness reflects classic information asymmetry and attribution biases in organizational hierarchies. When skill recognition depends on manager observation rather than systematic assessment, proximity bias and visibility bias determine career advancement more than actual competence.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should invest in centralized, continuous skills mapping infrastructure rather than relying on periodic reviews and manager assumptions. Treating internal talent visibility as a systems design problem—not a managerial awareness problem—directly reduces turnover costs and external hiring spend while improving internal mobility.

The data-driven gap between manager and employee perceptions is a textbook example of self-serving bias in leadership assessment. The practical recommendations align with evidence-based talent management research.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most companies say they&#8217;re invested in their people. Yet many employees experience something different.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">According to the<a href="https://www.talentlms.com/research/skills-visibility-report"> TalentLMS Skills Visibility Report</a>, four in 10 employees say it&#8217;s easier to find a new job elsewhere than a new role within their own company. That finding points to something larger than the state of the job market. Too many organizations struggle to see and activate the talent they already have.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Here&#8217;s what I believe is at the root of it: Companies don&#8217;t have a talent shortage, but rather a talent visibility problem. As CEO of Epignosis, the parent company of TalentLMS, I&#8217;ve spent years working with organizations on workforce learning, skills development, and talent growth. One challenge comes up again and again: Companies often know far less about the skills within their own workforce than they think they do. <a href="https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-09-18-gartner-hr-research-finds-orgs-current-talent-management-efforts-inhibit-optimal-employee-and-org-performance">Gartner research</a> suggests that only 8% of companies have reliable data on the skills their workforce already has.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When skills go unseen, so do the people who have them.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-gap-few-organizations-fully-recognize">The gap few organizations fully recognize</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">At TalentLMS, we help organizations train, upskill, and develop their workforce through our learning management system. For our Skills Visibility Report, we surveyed 1,500 U.S. employees and managers about skills in the workplace. One of the most striking findings wasn&#8217;t about what people can do. It was about whether organizations notice.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Fully 90% of managers say they understand their team&#8217;s skills. Only 69% of employees agree. That&#8217;s a 21-point perception gap, and it shapes everything that follows.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The disconnect goes deeper. Again, 90% of managers say they support their teams in developing new skills. Only 60% of employees feel they actually receive that support. The people making development decisions believe the system works. The people on the receiving end aren&#8217;t so sure.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I don&#8217;t believe this reflects a lack of good intentions. Most managers genuinely want their people to succeed. The problem is structural. When skills aren&#8217;t tracked in a consistent, transparent way, perception replaces proof. Decisions about who gets opportunities, promotions, or stretch projects can become influenced by who is visible rather than who is capable.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-when-staying-feels-like-standing-still">When staying feels like standing still</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The consequences show up most clearly in internal mobility.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">More than half of employees—56%—say their career progression has stalled at some point because their skills went unnoticed. Meanwhile, 42% say their manager addresses workforce skill gaps only after performance has taken a hit.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Consider the employee&#8217;s perspective. You&#8217;re growing. You&#8217;re learning on the job. You&#8217;re building new capabilities every week. But none of that is visible to the people who make decisions about your career.<strong> </strong>Over time, it&#8217;s easy to see why employees would look elsewhere for opportunities to grow.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And that&#8217;s exactly what&#8217;s happening. Nearly a third of employees say they would consider leaving their company due to a lack of skill development opportunities.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Quitting isn&#8217;t always about dissatisfaction. Often, it&#8217;s a logical response to a system that doesn&#8217;t surface what people can do.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-real-cost-of-invisible-skills">The real cost of invisible skills</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There&#8217;s a downstream effect that makes this even more expensive. Half of the people we surveyed say their company regularly hires externally for skills that already exist internally.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Companies are spending time and money recruiting talent from outside while the people already on the team watch their own skills go unused. Every time I hear a leader say, &#8220;We just can&#8217;t find the right people,” I want to ask a different question: Have you looked—really looked—at the people you already have?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When organizations don&#8217;t have a clear, shared view of what skills exist across their workforce, they default to external <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/hiring" title="Hiring">hiring</a>.<a href="https://business.linkedin.com/learn/resources/workplace-learning-report"> LinkedIn&#8217;s 2025 Workplace Learning Report</a> found that 88% of organizations are concerned about employee retention. And providing learning opportunities is their number one retention strategy. Yet our findings suggest that learning creates the most value when organizations can recognize and activate the capabilities it develops. When they can&#8217;t, internal mobility stalls. Talent walks out the door. And the cycle repeats.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The challenge isn&#8217;t a lack of talent or even a lack of learning opportunities. It&#8217;s whether leaders have built systems that make skills visible and connect growth to opportunity. As the leadership development expert Julie Winkle Giulioni echoes in our report: &#8220;When it&#8217;s easier to leave than to grow, that&#8217;s not a retention issue, it&#8217;s a leadership one.”</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-has-to-change-for-internal-mobility-to-work">What has to change for internal mobility to work</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most organizations already have internal mobility programs. The problem is that those programs sit on top of broken infrastructure. You can launch internal job boards and encourage lateral moves, but if the underlying system still depends on managers’ assumptions about what every person on their team can do, you&#8217;re building on sand.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Three things need to fundamentally shift.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>1. How skills are measured</strong></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Nearly half of our respondents—47%—say visibility would improve with regular skills assessments or audits. That means taking skill identification out of the annual review cycle and treating it as a living, continuous process. When managers are no longer the sole source of truth about what someone can do, the entire system becomes less subjective and harder to distort.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>2. How skills connect to movement</strong></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Employees need to see their path: What should I build, where will it take me, and how do I get there from where I am now? When those answers are visible, people invest in growth inside the company rather than investing in their exit.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>3. Infrastructure </strong></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Today, only 18% of organizations use a dedicated platform to track skills. The rest rely on performance reviews, manager observation, and self-reporting—methods that are inconsistent, incomplete, and difficult to surface across an organization. A centralized <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence" title="AI">AI</a> skills mapping system should make skills visible across teams and create a shared source of truth for managers, HR, and employees.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-leadership-question">The leadership question</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Every company has people ready to grow. The question leaders need to answer is whether they&#8217;ve built an organization that can see them.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When people feel seen, they stay. When they feel invisible, they leave. And in a market where finding talent is hard enough, losing the people you already have because you couldn&#8217;t see what they bring is a cost no organization should accept.</p>
