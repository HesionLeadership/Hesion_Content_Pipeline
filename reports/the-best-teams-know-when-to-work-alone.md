# The Best Teams Know When to Work Alone

## Summary
HBR explores how the best-performing teams strategically alternate between collaborative work and independent work, rather than defaulting to constant collaboration.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
Research on team effectiveness shows that intermittent collaboration—cycling between solo deep work and group interaction—produces better outcomes than continuous teamwork, challenging the assumption that more collaboration always equals better performance.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should design work structures that intentionally build in periods of independent work alongside collaboration, rather than defaulting to open-plan, always-on team environments. Leaders need to recognize that protecting individual focus time is a team performance strategy, not an anti-team behavior.
