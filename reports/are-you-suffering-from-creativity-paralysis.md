# Are you suffering from creativity paralysis?

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-22T08:00:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91570272/are-you-suffering-from-creativity-paralysis](https://www.fastcompany.com/91570272/are-you-suffering-from-creativity-paralysis)

## Summary
The article introduces the concept of 'creativity paralysis'—being stuck at either the ideation or execution extreme—and offers a framework (wonder vs. rigor stages, prototype mindset, and strategic completion) for leaders to overcome it.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
Creativity paralysis maps onto well-studied constructs like perfectionism, action-state orientation, and the tension between exploration and exploitation in organizations. Understanding which phase of the creative process individuals and teams are in can help leaders design appropriate structures and psychological safety to maintain momentum.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should help leaders and teams diagnose whether they are stuck in endless ideation or over-refinement, then apply stage-appropriate interventions: protect early-stage ideas from premature process constraints, use prototyping and deadlines to drive execution, and normalize 'good enough' completion to free capacity for the next initiative.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">We&#8217;ve all heard of analysis paralysis—that maddening loop where overthinking prevents any decision from being made. But in a recent podcast conversation with Beri Meric, founder of Ivy, I was asked a really great question: What about creativity paralysis? The more I sat with the question, the more I realized it&#8217;s one of the most underdiagnosed <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> problems leaders face today.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I define creativity as the toggle between wonder and rigor—the balance of open-ended imagination and disciplined execution that I call WonderRigor. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Creativity paralysis happens at either extreme. Spend too long in the wonder field of blue-sky thinking and you never gain traction. Over-index on rigor—the obsessive refinement, the endless specialization—and you become so myopic you lose sight of the forest for the trees. Either way, you&#8217;re stuck.</p>




<p></p>


<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A recent conversation with Kathy Oneto, executive coach and author of <em>Sustainable Ambition</em>, helped me see how ambition itself follows a creative process—and why so many leaders never finish what they start. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Oneto defines ambition as goals for which we carry motivation, a level of aspiration, and the commitment that guides our priorities and effort. But she&#8217;s quick to note that ambition is not only a trait; it&#8217;s also a state that ebbs and flows. And when we don&#8217;t honor that, we get paralyzed.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-1-know-your-stage-and-respect-it">1. Know your stage—and respect it</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The antidote to creativity paralysis begins with honest self-assessment. Are you in the wonder stage, where the idea still needs oxygen and space? Or are you in the rigor stage, where execution requires structure and deadlines? Conflating the two is where paralysis breeds. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">You don&#8217;t always want to rush the wonder stage because premature rigor kills nascent ideas. But once you&#8217;ve crossed into execution, constraints become creative fuel. As I&#8217;ve shared again and again from the stage: Creativity loves constraints. A deadline isn&#8217;t the enemy of imagination. It&#8217;s the invitation to commit.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-2-treat-it-as-a-prototype-not-a-masterpiece">2. Treat it as a prototype, not a masterpiece</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">One of the most liberating reframes for creativity paralysis is to treat your output as a prototype. Oneto reminded me that in complex systems, the goal isn&#8217;t to be right; it&#8217;s to learn. When you&#8217;re running an experiment rather than unveiling a finished work, you become less precious. You move. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This maps directly to how my Move. Think. Rest. (MTR) framework operates as infrastructure for creative leadership: Motion—literal and figurative—breaks logjams. The PhD adviser who told me &#8220;Hopefully this will not be your <em>best</em> piece of work!&#8221; gave me permission to finish the research and avoid making perfection the enemy of good. That permission is the prototype mindset.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-3-completion-is-not-surrender-it-s-strategy">3. Completion is not surrender‚ it&#8217;s strategy</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Perhaps the hardest move in any creative endeavor is declaring it complete. After finishing her book, Oneto found herself in what she calls her &#8220;ambition-in-between stage&#8221;—a liminal space many leaders recognize but rarely name. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">She turned to creative texts for guidance and landed on what choreographer Twyla Tharp describes as the container: the discipline of saying “This is done, and it is fine for now.” </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Elizabeth Gilbert put it plainly in <em>Big Magic</em>: At some point you must finish your work and release it, if only so you can go on to make other things with a glad and determined heart. Rick Rubin echoes this—we finish the current project in service to the next one.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This is what I think of as the deepest insight about creativity paralysis: It isn&#8217;t just about being stuck. It&#8217;s about not knowing how to let go. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The MTR framework offers one on-ramp: Rest isn&#8217;t passive; it&#8217;s the active replenishment that allows the toggle between wonder and rigor to remain fluid. Step away, zoom out, and cultivate the inputs that will fuel your next idea. You can&#8217;t generate from an empty well.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">What I call the Imagination Era—a moment when human creativity and sentient intelligence are the scarcest and most strategically valuable assets an organization can cultivate—demands that we bring our full creative capacity to work, not just our analytical horsepower. That means diagnosing the specific form of paralysis that&#8217;s slowing you down, giving yourself the structure of a stage, the grace of a prototype, and the courage of completion. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Your next great idea is waiting on the other side of the one you haven&#8217;t yet let go.</p>
