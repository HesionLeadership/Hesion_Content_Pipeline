# Women and men in the C-suite see 2 different workplaces

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-17T19:15:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91575943/women-and-men-in-the-c-suite-see-two-different-workplaces](https://www.fastcompany.com/91575943/women-and-men-in-the-c-suite-see-two-different-workplaces)

## Summary
A 60-year longitudinal HBR survey reveals a widening perception gap between male and female C-suite executives: 90% of women believe they are judged more critically in executive roles versus 35% of men, and 83% of women feel they must be more exceptional to succeed compared to 28% of men.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The growing divergence in how men and women perceive fairness, meritocracy, and evaluation standards at the executive level reflects intensifying 'prove-it-again' bias and highlights a critical failure in organizational sensemaking—leaders in the same company are experiencing fundamentally different realities, which undermines trust, engagement, and retention of senior women.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should not assume that formal equality in policies translates to experienced equality; they should conduct perception audits segmented by gender (and other demographics) to identify gaps in how promotion criteria, sponsorship, and performance evaluation are actually experienced, then redesign talent processes with transparent, evidence-based criteria to close those gaps.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Every 20 years since 1965, <em>Harvard Business Review</em> <a href="https://hbr.org/2026/07/what-60-years-of-data-reveals-about-how-men-and-women-experience-leadership?tpcc=orgsocial_edit&amp;utm_campaign=hbr&amp;utm_medium=social&amp;utm_source=facebook,linkedin,twitter" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">has published</a> an article with the results of a survey asking men and <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/91572939/the-workplace-isnt-designed-for-older-women" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">women</a> executives nearly identical questions about the perception of women in executive roles.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In 1965, 2,000 executives were surveyed, of which a “substantially large” portion of men viewed women in management roles negatively, because they viewed the executive suite as an inappropriate place for women to be.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Fast-forward 60 years. Researchers conducted a new iteration of the study, surveying 193 men and women senior executives in the U.S. The survey shows attitudes improved greatly between 1965 and 2006—but since then, a gap has surfaced in how men and women view opportunities, standards, and evaluation in the workplace.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In 2006, men and women answered nearly identically (35%) to a question asking whether women were judged more critically in executive roles. Now, men still answer at 35%, but that number jumped to 90% among women.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Meanwhile, 83% of women believe they need to be “more exceptional” than men to succeed, compared with just 28% of men. In 2006, 68% of women and 32% of men agreed with that view. The researchers state these findings are indicative that the “prove-it again” bias—in which women and people of color must provide more evidence of competence to be viewed equally as capable—has intensified.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Of the women surveyed, 37% believed promotion criteria are equal across genders, versus 70% of men. Even more, just 40% of women believe their company is a meritocracy compared with 76% of men.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The study further confirmed the double standard that men are promoted to senior-level roles based on the promise of their future, whereas women have to provide pristine records to make the cut.&nbsp;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">According to the researchers, men attributed this to the demands of senior-level roles hindering the flexibility that <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/91541720/corporate-america-is-crushing-senior-level-mothers" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">parents of young children</a> may want. Women, on the other hand, said the assumption was applied to women before they were asked.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">While attitudes have improved since the first survey in 1965, the perception gap has widened. In recent years, political polarization has led to the <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/91324404/how-trump-has-gutted-dei-in-his-first-100-days" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">rollback</a> of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives. </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Lean In and McKinsey’s 2025 <a href="https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/women-in-the-workplace#/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">“Women in the Workplace” study</a> found women were sponsored less often than men—and even when they had sponsors, they were promoted less frequently. The study also shows that for 11 consecutive years, women have been underrepresented at every level of the corporate pipeline. In senior leadership, women accounted for just 29% of C-suite roles. And those women in senior-level roles have struggled with higher rates of burnout and a more difficult climb to the top.</p>
