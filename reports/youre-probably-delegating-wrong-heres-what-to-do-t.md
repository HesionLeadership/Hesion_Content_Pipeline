# You’re probably delegating wrong. Here’s what to do to fix that

**Source:** Fast Company  
**Published:** 2026-07-20T09:55:00  
**URL:** [https://www.fastcompany.com/91569517/are-you-delegating-wrong-delegating-leadership-management](https://www.fastcompany.com/91569517/are-you-delegating-wrong-delegating-leadership-management)

## Summary
The article argues that leaders should reframe delegation from merely offloading tasks to a developmental practice that builds employees' high-value skills, confidence, and career growth through clear expectations, specific deadlines, structured check-ins, and after-action reviews.

## Organizational Psychology Angle
The piece draws on self-determination theory (autonomy, mastery, purpose) and psychological safety principles, arguing that delegation failures stem from unclear context, power dynamics that inhibit question-asking, and treating delegation as task transfer rather than capability building.

## Leadership Lesson
CHROs and COOs should train managers to treat every delegated assignment as a dual-purpose opportunity—delivering the immediate deliverable while explicitly developing the employee's skills—by setting clear expectations, establishing structured check-ins, and conducting after-action reviews that include honest reflection on the leader's own effectiveness.

## Original Story Summary
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Too many leaders approach delegation only as an opportunity to transfer work off their plate. The challenge with this is they often find themselves disappointed and let down when the quality of the work falls short of expectations, the project is late, and important details are missed.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Frustrated, these leaders conclude, &#8220;I should have just done it myself.&#8221;</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I believe delegation can be so much more. Delegation is an opportunity to help people learn career-building, pride-inducing, high-value skills. In other words, delegation builds capability that lasts long after the project ends.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And this approach has other benefits. As <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/artificial-intelligence" title="AI">AI</a> increasingly handles routine work, a leader&#8217;s competitive advantage is no longer simply getting more work done or becoming more strategic. It&#8217;s developing people who can think critically, collaborate effectively, exercise judgment, and lead others. Delegation has become one of the most important ways to build those critical capabilities.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">So, let’s explore the approach where delegation becomes something much better for everyone.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-step-1-help-others-learn-high-value-skills">Step 1. Help Others Learn High-Value Skills</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most employees genuinely want to do good work. They want to contribute, grow, and succeed.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Daniel Pink helped popularize the idea that people perform better when they understand how their work connects to a larger purpose. This means that while the conversation focuses on the project, it should also focus on what unique skills or lived experiences the employee has that makes them a great fit, and what growth opportunities you see. As part of the conversation, also ask:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>What skills would you like to strengthen?</li>



<li>What kind of projects interest you?</li>



<li>Where would additional support help?</li>



<li>What would make this assignment meaningful for you?</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">To truly inspire your employees, help them embrace projects as investments in their future rather than simply more work.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-step-2-share-expectations-and-purpose">Step 2. Share Expectations and Purpose</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Context creates ownership. This next step in delegation should create results today while continuing to build repeatable capability and confidence.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When employees fall short of expectations, it is often because they do not have the same context as their leader. What feels obvious to the leader may be invisible to the employee. When employees don’t receive clear direction, they fill in the gaps with assumptions—and that is where projects will begin to drift.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Employees may also hesitate to ask for clarity. They may worry that asking for more information will make them seem unprepared, inexperienced, or irresponsible. Power dynamics, cultural norms, or workplace expectations can make asking questions feel risky . . . so employees don’t ask.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Inspired leaders recognize and minimize these risks by having respectful, direct conversations about expectations early. This means that before assigning the work, leaders explain why a task matters, what&#8217;s at stake, who will be affected, and what outstanding performance looks like. Sharing expectations and purpose allows employees to make better decisions without constantly returning for direction.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-step-3-set-specific-deadlines">Step 3. Set Specific deadlines</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Using language that feels clear but often isn’t, is one of the most common reasons leaders and their teams have challenges. For example, we use:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>ASAP.</li>



<li>When you get a chance.</li>



<li>Sometime next week.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Different people hear those phrases differently. Saying &#8220;Thursday at 3:00 p.m.” removes ambiguity and sets clear expectations. This also gives the employee opportunity to discuss competing priorities with their leader and practice effective <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/section/productivity" title="Productivity">time management</a>.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-step-4-agree-on-how-you-ll-stay-connected">Step 4. Agree On How You&#8217;ll Stay Connected</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Delegation should not mean stepping away. I recommend giving your employees autonomy while staying connected. Agree upon official checkpoints before work begins. For example, will there be:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Weekly or biweekly updates?</li>



<li>A midpoint review?</li>



<li>Milestone check-ins?</li>



<li>A final presentation?</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This goal isn&#8217;t micromanagement; it’s about supporting employees and preventing surprises. Supportive check-ins create opportunities to course-correct early rather than lose time, opportunity, and employee morale later in the project.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-step-5-review-the-project-and-your-leadership">Step 5. Review the Project— and Your Leadership</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A project is not complete when the deliverable is finished. The final step is reflection and exploring how the project can be performed better the next time, the team’s performance, and the leader&#8217;s own leadership.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">An after-action review (AAR) is a structured debrief used to examine the mission, what happened, and why it happened, and figure out what should change going forward. Long used in the military, it helps teams turn experience into practical lessons.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A useful AAR brings together everyone involved as soon as possible while the details are still fresh. The discussion should capture the goals, successes, issues, and delays in a safe, growth-oriented environment. It should end with clear lessons learned and specific recommendations for what to do differently next time.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">To prepare for an AAR, get ready to ask questions like:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>What worked well?</li>



<li>What obstacles emerged?</li>



<li>How could I have delegated more effectively?</li>



<li>What additional support would have helped?</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And for each employee involved in the project, ask them what they would like more exposure to in the future, which connects back to Step 1.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-conclusion-delegation-is-about-development-and-legacies-that-endure">Conclusion: Delegation Is About Development and Legacies That Endure</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When delegation becomes development, that’s when employees grow, teams become more capable, and leaders create the capacity to focus on the strategic challenges. Instead of being the bottleneck, leaders become people whom employees choose to follow and support because they know the leader is supporting them.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The organizations that thrive over the next decade won&#8217;t simply be the ones that work faster. They&#8217;ll also be the ones that develop capable, flexible, collaborative, and loyal teams. And as a bonus, delegation gives leaders time to develop their own skills.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Every assignment is an opportunity to complete a project—or to build a future leader. The best leaders know that doing both is the more valuable outcome.</p>
