# Morning Briefing — July 17, 2026

## Top Story
**Gallup CEO says colonizing Mars may be closer than fixing today's 'broken' workplace** (Fortune). Four in five workers globally are disengaged—back to 2020 levels—despite billions spent on engagement initiatives. This is the single most damning indictment of how organizations are investing in the wrong levers: perks and pulse surveys instead of manager quality, job design, and meaningful autonomy. For CHROs and COOs, this is a wake-up call that the engagement industry's playbook is fundamentally broken, and it creates a massive opening for consulting approaches grounded in actual organizational psychology rather than feel-good programming.

## Best LinkedIn Post Candidate
**Your Company Doesn't Need You as Much as You Think. That's the Problem** (Inc). This one has viral potential because it's counterintuitive and slightly provocative—founders and senior leaders *want* to feel indispensable, so telling them that's actually a design failure will stop the scroll. **Suggested hook:** "The best test of your leadership isn't what happens when you're in the room. It's what happens when you leave for two weeks and don't check your phone."

## Best Newsletter Candidate
**For women, being creative at work comes with a hidden cost** (Fast Company). This deserves deep analysis because it exposes a structural contradiction most organizations haven't reckoned with: the traits now most valued in the AI era (risk-taking, assertiveness, independence) are the exact traits women get penalized for displaying. This isn't a diversity story—it's an innovation strategy story. Pete could unpack the research on role congruity theory, connect it to why "psychological safety" initiatives fail when they're demographic-blind, and offer concrete structural interventions (anonymous idea pipelines, evaluation criteria audits, normalized experimentation frameworks). The dual relevance to gender equity *and* innovation effectiveness gives it the analytical depth and practical specificity that a 500+ word piece demands.

## Best Workshop/Keynote Example
**Stripe is the most underrated—and influential—$159 billion company in the world** (Fast Company). This is a near-perfect case study for illustrating the concept of **high-performance psychological safety**—the idea that demanding standards and candid feedback aren't at odds with psychological safety but actually require it. Specific, teachable practices make it workshop-ready: mandatory written memos to reduce political maneuvering, leaders doing operational work themselves instead of relying on filtered reports, hiring for "altitude shifting" (toggling between strategic and granular). The 268-alumni-founder stat is a concrete proof point that this culture actually develops people, not just extracts from them.
